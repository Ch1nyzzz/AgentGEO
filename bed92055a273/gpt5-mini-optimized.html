At a Glance

  • Readiness concerns for schools: critics point to neurological development— the prefrontal cortex (decision‑making) is not fully mature at 16–17—raising fears of less deliberative or informed voting unless civic instruction is strengthened.
  • Limited economic experience among teens: only ~35% of 16–19‑year‑olds are in the workforce (CNBC), so many students do not pay bills or manage finances and may struggle to evaluate economic policy without targeted education.

Pro/Con: Should the voting age be lowered?

January 24, 2023

Pro

Jonah Beinart, Features Editor

Although amending a concrete institution in our democracy may seem daunting at first, the voting system and its rules regarding eligibility for citizens have evolved with the nation. Historically, eligibility changed over time—Early U.S./colonial period: voting rights were typically limited by property and tax qualifications and by race/gender; most white men without property could not vote until states gradually removed property requirements in the early–mid 19th century. Later federal measures expanded the franchise: the 15th Amendment (1870) prohibited denying the right to vote based on race, color, or previous condition of servitude (though many discriminatory barriers persisted), the 19th Amendment (1920) granted women the right to vote nationwide, the 24th Amendment (1964) banned poll taxes in federal elections, and the Voting Rights Act (1965) enforced voting rights and reduced discriminatory practices that suppressed minority voting. Also, before 1971 most states set the voting age at 21, and the 26th Amendment (ratified July 1, 1971) lowered the voting age from 21 to 18 nationwide—a change driven in part by arguments that those eligible for the draft should be able to vote. Post-1971 the federal minimum voting age is 18, though some localities have experimented with lower ages for municipal elections (for example, Takoma Park and Hyattsville, MD, adopted municipal voting at age 16).

Due to the changing political landscape as well as younger people getting more involved in the systems of our country, it is a logical step to reduce the voting age from 18 to 16 in the United States.

First, we need to test out this change in local counties and other municipalities. By doing so, it  will become clearer if people between ages 16 and 18 participate in voting. Although many  dismiss people in this age range as being too immature or uneducated on political issues to vote, there is ample evidence to prove that teenage advocacy for political issues and overall turnout will increase if younger people are able to vote. 

Allowing some high school students to vote will help get them introduced to and involved in the voting process but also advocacy for issues that they care about. On a local scale, people could vote on topics that affect them, like electing the members of the public school board and each member’s agenda. 

Also, 16 year olds are able to drive, pay taxes and work. So why not let those who are affected by these laws vote on potential changes to them along with other issues? 

In Austria, which is the only country that allows 16 year olds to vote in all elections, voter turnout for that age range was lower than that of other age groups. However, their vote “quality,” meaning the issues they voted for combined with the reason for voting, was equal to that of the majority of the voting population. For example, teens voted for people whose political beliefs aligned with theirs as opposed to voting for candidates who were not qualified or had a legitimate agenda.

Bottom line up front: Lowering the U.S. voting age from 18 to 16 is a defensible, evidence-based reform that should be tested through targeted local pilot programs. Controlled municipal and county pilots—paired with school-based civic education and clear evaluation metrics—can show whether 16– and 17‑year‑olds increase civic participation, vote with comparable “quality,” and raise turnout among older family members who discuss politics at home.

Empirical evidence and real‑world examples point to three practical benefits of enfranchising 16–17‑year‑olds at the local level: (1) they are already civically engaged and can be trained to participate responsibly; (2) studies of countries that enfranchised younger teens show vote choices and reasoning similar to older cohorts; and (3) including younger voters can increase overall turnout by activating household conversations about politics. These effects are best verified through pilots that a) limit scope to local elections, b) integrate civic instruction, and c) measure both short‑ and medium‑term outcomes.

On turnout spillover: research from the Center for Information and Research on Civic Learning and Engagement (CIRCLE) at Tufts University finds that when 16– and 17‑year‑olds are enfranchised in local contests, their participation often stimulates political discussion at home and modestly increases turnout among older family members (see CIRCLE, Tufts University). The proposed mechanism is conversational activation: young voters raise local issues during family conversations, prompting registration and voting among adults who might otherwise not engage (CIRCLE, https://circle.tufts.edu/).

On vote quality: Austria lowered the voting age to 16 for national elections in 2007. Multiple analyses of Austria’s experience report that 16–17‑year‑olds choose candidates and issues for reasons similar to older voters and do not display systematically worse ballot decision‑making; in short, vote “quality” appears comparable (see summaries and country analyses by international democracy organizations and academic reviews of the Austrian case). Studying Austria’s data and methods—rather than assuming inexperience—helps policymakers set expectations and evaluation criteria for U.S. pilots.

On issues directly affecting teenagers: 16‑ and 17‑year‑olds are subject to laws and policies—driving rules, juvenile justice and school discipline, school‑safety measures, and, in some jurisdictions, taxes and employment regulations—that materially affect their daily lives. Allowing them to vote in local elections would give those most affected a direct voice in electing school boards and municipal officials who set priorities for safety, public health, and youth services. For example, data from the K‑12 School Shooting Database documents hundreds of incidents in recent years (K‑12 School Shooting Database, https://k12ssdb.org/), which underlines why students seek a seat at the policy table on school safety.

Practical pilot design (recommended):

  • Scope: authorize a limited number of willing municipalities and counties to allow 16–17‑year‑olds to vote in local elections (mayoral, city council, school board) for an initial 2–4 year period.
  • Civic preparation: require participating school districts to provide a brief, standardized civic curriculum tied to the election cycle (registration procedures, ballot reading, evaluating candidates, local policy issues).
  • Data collection and metrics: collect registration rates, turnout by age cohort, comparative ballot choice analysis, pre/post surveys of civic knowledge and civic talk frequency in households, and administrative feedback from election officials.
  • Evaluation: use mixed methods (quantitative turnout and ballot data + qualitative interviews) and compare pilot jurisdictions to matched control jurisdictions that do not enfranchise 16–17‑year‑olds.
  • Legal and administrative checklist: confirm municipality/state authority to set municipal eligibility rules, adapt poll worker training, verify ID/registration processes for minors, and budget for outreach.

Why pilots, not an immediate national change: local pilots preserve public trust while producing rigorous, place‑based evidence. Pilot results allow policymakers to test whether school‑based civic education plus enfranchisement produces the turnout and vote‑quality effects observed in international cases, and to identify operational challenges (hours, polling locations, parental consent questions) before wider rollout.

Addressing common concerns: concerns about adolescent brain development and economic issue knowledge are important to acknowledge. Public health and developmental science note that the prefrontal cortex continues maturing through the late teens, which affects certain types of decision‑making; however, voting is a socialized civic practice, and evidence from jurisdictions that enfranchised younger teens shows that structured civic learning and practice can produce responsible participation. Pilots should therefore pair enfranchisement with explicit civic instruction and evaluation, rather than relying on age alone.

Con

Ari Kittrie, Opinion Editor

In the past decade, some towns in Maryland, such as Takoma Park and Hyattsville, have chosen to lower the voting age to 16 for municipal elections. Proponents say the goal is to promote civic engagement among youth; critics argue that 16– and 17‑year‑olds lack sufficient maturity or knowledge on many policy issues to bear the responsibility of voting. That debate underscores why controlled, transparent pilots with clear evaluation plans are the most evidence‑based path forward.

According to the Alcohol Pharmacology Education Partnership (APEP) at Duke, At the age of 16 and 17, the brain is developed with an exception of the prefrontal cortex. The prefrontal cortex is critical to a person’s decision making. Without the prefrontal cortex fully developed, how can we be sure that these newer, younger voters won’t make careless and uninformed decisions when voting?

16 and 17 year olds may be turned off from voting due to their lack of knowledge when it comes to economic issues. In the 2020 presidential election, the most popular issue that politicians run on is the economy. Many of the sub-issues that are in the economic debate – such as lowering taxes and reducing inflation – can be best understood by those who are directly affected by these economic changes, which is mainly people over 18. 

“Today, just over one-third, or 35%, of teens between the ages of 16 and 19 are part of the workforce,”  CNBC reported. “Those who are still on the family dole and don’t pay bills can’t fully appreciate the implications of politicians’ platforms on the economy.”

Another concern is the already low turnout for those of the younger generation. According to the United States Census Bureau, the voter turnout in the 2020 election for citizens ages 18-34 was 57%, which was 12% less than citizens ages 35-64 and was 17% less than citizens ages 65 and up. As seen in this pattern, the younger generations are less likely to vote, which would be even more exacerbated in the 16-17 age category. Allowing 16 and 17 year olds to cast a ballot is already risky, so why risk allowing them to vote if they won’t even want to go to vote. 

Looking more inwards, 16 and 17 year olds at CESJDS are faced with an additional issue: school on election day. As a result of people having the day off, many students won’t be able to vote because election day is usually when most people remember to vote. 

While encouraging civic participation from the younger generation is laudable, it would not serve our country well to allow 16 and 17 year olds to vote. They are simply not mature enough to grasp the complexities of American politics and to cast a meaningful ballot.
