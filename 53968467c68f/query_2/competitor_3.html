Seven Families, Seven Journeys

A Story of Urban Life in India

by Elizabeth Hendley images by Eugene Lee

Humanity has always been on the move. Across centuries and around the world, migration in response to immediate need changes families for generations.

Over the last century, urban migration has shaped India’s present and future. Explosive economic growth — sparked by fiscal reforms, foreign investment, and a technology boom — propelled millions from rural villages to cities in search of a better life.

814,200,000

By 2050, India is projected to add 404 million urban dwellers — more than the entire population of the U.S. — to the 410.2 million already living in its cities.

Source: U.N.

As they fuel the economic engines of India’s major cities, those who relocate often trade one set of challenges for another. City dwellers breathe polluted air and are at risk for violence, but they gain upward mobility and the chance to confront cultural traditions.

Children are the first to thrive — or suffer — in a new environment, even if the choice to migrate was made in their interest. Are they better off in cities, with more opportunities within reach? Can historical patterns of poverty be broken in this context?

Meet families in two cities navigating this balance.

Kumar Family

“Society thinks girls are a burden.”

— Solenka Kumar

Kashish Kumar

5 years old
Estimated amount parents will pay in dowries: $773 per daughter
Distance traveled: 331 miles
Started: Ganjapur, Rajasthan
Ended: Agra, Uttar Pradesh

At the edge of a busy main road in Agra, Solenka Kumar’s pushcart vies for the attention of passing pedestrians. She sells a rainbow of colorful bangles, and business is good as women purchase new accessories ahead of the Diwali festival.

Cars, rickshaws, cows, tuk-tuks, and bicycles all jockey for position on the crowded street while Solenka’s small daughters dart around their mother’s pushcart, dangerously close to the traffic.

It’s just after 2 p.m., and the girls, Kashish, 5, and Ani, 4, have finished their class at the anganwadi. Too young to be in school, their options for the afternoon are to play near their mother’s cart as traffic whizzes by or play at home, where there’s no supervision.

Neither option is entirely safe. Solenka and her husband, Vinod Kumar, 33, live in Agra’s Nagla Mohan neighborhood with their four young daughters. Narrow alleys separate multi-story buildings that crowd out the sunlight.

They live in a rented space on the second floor of a house — a small, bright blue room where the family of six sleeps together on one bed, along with concrete outdoor space for cooking.

Kashish is still sleeping in the family’s bed as her sisters and parents start the day. The bedroom, just big enough for a bed, storage cabinet, and small refrigerator, opens out into the outdoor living space.
The family spends most of their time in the home’s outdoor area, where they cook, bathe, and play. Cooking on a propane gas stove, Solenka makes breakfast for her daughters and packs a lunch for Vinod.
In a bricked-off corner of the outdoor living area, Vinod shaves before heading to work in the shoemaking factory. There’s no running water, but they bring up buckets of water to brush teeth, shave, and bathe.
It’s play time for Kashish after her father leaves for work and her eldest sister goes to school. The short brick wall (left) hasn’t always been there — her parents added it after Kashish fell off the edge to the street one story below.
The family’s densely populated neighborhood in the heart of Agra doesn’t allow for much personal space or privacy.
On the streets below, cows wander through the neighborhood’s narrow alleys, which are lined with drainage ditches.

Vinod moved from Ganjapur, Rajasthan, in 2002 in hope of finding a better job. He worked as a mason there, and Agra’s shoemaking industry held the promise of better work and higher wages. He splits his time at two shoemaking factories and earns between 50 to 200 rupees a day (US 75 cents to $3).

Solenka, 31, grew up just 20 minutes from Nagla Mohan in a slightly more affluent family. Her uncle arranged her marriage to Vinod when she was 20, and she joined her husband in Agra.

The marriage was a “difficult adjustment for me,” says Solenka. “When I was growing up, I was unlimited. But then I had to run a house on little [money] — I went from a lot to a little.”

Their financial troubles multiplied with each daughter they had. Though their four girls are still young — only the eldest, 11-year-old Kamya, is old enough to be in school — Vinod and Solenka already worry about the four dowries they’ll be responsible for.

For 12 hours a day, Vinod stands at his workstation in one of Agra’s many shoemaking factories and constructs shoes by hand, beginning with cutting pieces from patterns.

“As soon as a girl is born, you think about a dowry,” says Solenka. “Society thinks girls are a burden.”

For poor families, the looming reality of a dowry when a daughter is born can be especially daunting, but parents of all socioeconomic backgrounds spend years saving up for the jewelry, cash, household items, and even motorbikes they’re expected to give to their son-in-law’s family before a marriage takes place.

It’s one on a list of social and economic reasons why many families across India prefer sons over daughters, who are sometimes seen as burdens who don’t contribute financially to the household. This cultural belief can lead to child marriage — once a girl is married off, there is one less mouth to feed.

Vinod and Solenka had a son, born after eldest daughter Kamya. The baby lived eight days before he died of pneumonia.

“My mother-in-law thinks we should keep trying to have a son,” says Solenka, but each pregnancy has left her weaker. “In Indian society, there is a drive to have a boy.”

Vinod doesn’t share the same perspective, but his mother remains adamant, and her opinion carries weight.

Solenka serves a breakfast of flatbread, cauliflower, and other healthy vegetables to her three younger daughters. She learned how to boost the nutrition in her cooking at the anganwadi.

His wages are barely enough for the family to live on. But because Solenka is a member of a women’s savings group supported by World Vision, she qualified to receive the pushcart and an initial inventory of bangles to sell.

Vinod isn’t entirely supportive of his wife’s work. The same social norms that reinforce a preference for boys over girls also discourage women from working outside the home.

Solenka says he feared what people will think of him and his ability to provide for his family if they saw her working. But she doesn’t care if she’s seen as a disgrace. Like millions of parents across the globe, the couple does what they have to do to provide for their children.

She’s committed to providing a life for her daughters that more closely mirrors her own upbringing. “I always wanted to do something for my household,” she says, “and now I have the opportunity with the cart and bangles.”

Solenka plans to expand her business to include marigold garlands, used as traditional decorations at festivals and weddings, and sell in a different area with more foot traffic.

Her contribution to the household income alleviates some of their financial pressures — and the couple would have likely faced even more challenges if they had stayed in Ganjapur.

Vinod would be earning less as a mason than he does making shoes, and the opportunity for Solenka to generate extra income outside the home would have been nonexistent.

In the spot where her neighborhood meets a busy street, Solenka sets up the pushcart where she sells colorful bangles to passersby. When they’re not at the anganwadi, her younger daughters keep their mother company and playfully dart around the cart.
A trio of customers stops to examine Solenka’s merchandise. With the Diwali festival a few days away, many women are purchasing new clothes and jewelry.
Solenka received the pushcart and initial supply of bangles as part of World Vision’s economic empowerment program in Agra. She qualified because she’s part of a local savings group.

Now, their children’s needs are met — but that wasn’t always the case. Before Solenka was earning income with the pushcart, they couldn’t afford nutritious food for their daughters. When Kashish was 2 and a half, she became malnourished and was put on a feeding plan at the anganwadi to get her to a healthy weight.

It’s hard to imagine the playful 5-year-old was once listless and lethargic. Her personality perked up once she gained weight and her mother learned to cook more nutritious food, like the green vegetables she now adds to most meals.

Kashish now bounds around the neighborhood, full of energy and curiosity. She skips down the concrete paths of Nagla Mohan toward the busy street where Solenka stands with her pushcart.

Kashish bounds around the corner to the anganwadi with enthusiasm, a contrast to a few years ago when malnourishment depleted her energy.

Though they will likely produce dowries for their daughters, Vinod and Solenka are showing Kashish and her sisters a commitment to family, working hard in whatever ways they must to provide a better life for their children.

Growing up watching her mother work — outside of their house, interacting with customers, and making any money she can — models for Kashish a different life, one where she can make her own decisions instead of following society’s expectations of what girls should be.

Sholanki Family

“Education is providing a way to improve life.”

— Om Sholanki

Preeti Sholanki

9 years old
Time until high school graduation: 9 years
Distance traveled: 329 miles
Started: Dhoda, Rajasthan
Ended: Agra, Uttar Pradesh

It’s 7:30 a.m. in Agra, the city in northern India home to the Taj Mahal, and the first wave of tourists walked through the monument’s entrance gates an hour ago at sunrise. About a mile away, in a neighborhood called Shambu Nagar, 9-year-old Preeti Sholanki is finishing her breakfast.

Life spills out of Shambu Nagar’s simple mud-walled structures, topped with tarps or tin, and onto its bricked paths. Old men brush their teeth as chickens stroll by; women trade neighborhood gossip just yards from children bathing.

A medley of smells — flatbread studded with onions cooking over open fires, sewage sitting in the drainage canals that line the pathways — envelops the neighborhood as Preeti’s sisters help her get ready for school.

Her family is more than her parents and siblings; there are four households containing dozens of aunts, uncles, and cousins. All are descendants of Preeti’s great-great-grandparents who migrated to Agra from Dhoda, Rajasthan, about 150 years ago when drought and famine swept across western India.

Kaushul, wearing the traditional Rajasthan headscarf, washes dishes after a family meal. The tattoo on her arm is her husband’s name.
Preeti splashes her face with water from the family’s outdoor faucet before she heads to school for the day. With every year she’s in school, she’s less likely to marry early like her mother did.

As her ancestors packed their things to leave their homeland in the 1800s, they couldn’t have envisioned Preeti, generations later, packing her schoolbooks into a backpack. They left Rajasthan to survive. But today Preeti, her basic needs met, leaves home each morning in pursuit of much more.

After carefully pulling her hair into a ponytail and putting on her backpack, she joins her friends Kiran and Roti to walk to school. It’s about a 20-minute walk — first across a dirt field strewn with trash, then through a maze of streets — until the trio in tidy matching uniforms reaches the shady square where their government-run school sits.

Preeti walks hand in hand with her two best friends, Kiran and Roti, on their way to school. The young girls walk by themselves 20 minutes to reach school, passing through streets crowded with men.
They begin their journey by crossing a dirt field behind Preeti’s neighborhood.
Once they arrive at school, students gather in the small courtyard to recite the Indian national anthem.

“School life is a better life,” says Preeti.

“Education is providing a way to improve life,” says her father, Om Sholanki, 40. And for his family, there is room for improvement.

Om attended school through fifth grade. Like many families in Shambu Nagar, he earns a living by making and selling “hunters” — Indiana Jones-style leather whips — to tourists outside the Taj Mahal. Each one costs about 400 rupees (US$6.10) in materials and takes Om and his wife, Kaushul, six hours to make, and Om sells them for whatever price he can get. Sometimes it’s as little as 100 rupees (US$1.50); on a good day, he sells 10 whips and covers the cost of materials.

Handling leather made from cows, believed to be sacred in this majority Hindu society, makes the family outcasts — and keeps them in poverty’s grip.

Om braids leather scraps under a tree his mother planted outside the family’s home. It typically takes about six hours to make a whip from start to finish.
He buys leather scraps from the city’s many shoemaking factories and braids the scraps together to create the whips.
Kaushul and Om work together like many other families in their neighborhood. Each whip costs about 400 rupees (US$6.10) in materials to make.
Om barely sells enough to cover the cost of materials and provide for his family.
Om takes the finished products and sells to tourists at the market outside the Taj Mahal’s gates. On a good day, he sells 10 whips.

“Every day you dig a well, you drink,” he says, speaking of the daily struggle to provide for his family. He says that each time he interacts with a tourist, he’s reminded how education can take his children to a different level in life.

He didn’t always believe this to be the case. It was the combined effort of his sister, Leela, and World Vision volunteers that convinced Om of the importance of education.

Leela, 32, is one of two people in this neighborhood of about 800 who completed high school. She doesn’t work making whips like the rest of the family. Instead, she helps run the local anganwadi, teaching basic education to children who aren’t in school, as well as leading nutrition and health classes for pregnant and lactating mothers.

“Most of the younger generation is illiterate, and they can be taken advantage of,” says Leela. “I thought, 'How will change come in my community if I’m not investing in them?'”

Preeti’s aunt Leela is a member of the Jeevan Jyoti women’s savings group, along with various neighbors and relatives. Jeevan Jyoti means “life light,” says Leela. “We want to help each other, which is light, and we want to brighten our community.”

She knows the opportunities available to those who complete their education, and she wants that for the young people in Shambu Nagar. In five years of working at the anganwadi, Leela has helped enroll 44 children in formal school, including Preeti.

Leela says, “I might not live to see the change that will happen in the community” as a result of her efforts, but she’s already seen the change in her niece’s life.

“I want to be like my aunt,” says Preeti. The third-grader is off to a good start. Her favorite subjects at school are Hindi, math, and English, and she enjoys studying.

“I will study more than my aunt,” says Preeti with a shy smile. “School life is a better life.”

Taking advantage of every opportunity, Preeti goes to a World Vision-run remedial education center for an hour after school. About 30 children play educational games and complete math and language exercises — all designed to reinforce what they learn at school and fill the gaps of what isn’t taught. They also have some fun, laughing through games of hopscotch and hide and seek.

Preeti brushes her hair before leaving for school in the morning.
Leela, bottom left, boils tea for various cousins and aunts while talking on her smartphone.
The family now has a toilet, constructed by World Vision, right outside their home, allowing a bit of privacy in a neighborhood where there is little.
Preeti and a cousin laugh as they practice moves for a traditional dance from Rajasthan, their ancestral homeland.
At the World Vision-run remedial education center, Preeti is a top pupil. The group meets daily to play educational games that supplement what they learn in school.

Back at Preeti’s house, her older sisters, Kiran, 18, and Pushpa, 16, aren’t in school. They dropped out several years ago when many of their classmates did the same, and now they spend their days helping Kaushal around the home. But since Om and Kaushal have come to value education and the opportunities that come along with it, Om has hired a tutor for Kiran and Pushpa to get back up to speed and re-enroll in school.

The tutor’s fee — 250 rupees (US$3.87) per month — stretches the family’s finances. Money is a constant worry and challenge.

“I work on a daily basis, and I’m the only one earning,” Om says. “How can I save or invest?”

He wants more for his children than just survival, and his support of their education counts as a different kind of investment.

“Every parent wants a good home for their kids and an education. My desire is that they study. Our life is gone but we want a good life for our kids,” he says, echoing the attitude across centuries that spurred his great-grandparents to move to Agra.

“I like to see the Taj Mahal, but I have never been inside,” says Preeti. The monument is just across the river from her neighborhood, yet she and scores of others who see the white marble domes every day can’t afford to go inside.

Part of a good life in their culture entails his daughters making a good marriage, and poor families are more likely to marry off their daughters at a young age. But in this family, two factors lower Preeti’s risk of child marriage. Her parents didn’t marry as young teenagers — Kaushal was 18 when she married Om, who was 20 — and Preeti is in school with the full support of her parents.

With marriage far from her mind, she dreams about helping others and becoming a teacher, like her aunt.

“Once I grow up, I want to improve my community,” Preeti says.

For now, she’s like any other 9-year-old after school: dancing around her house with cousins and sisters, wondering what’s for dinner (she hopes it’s dal makhani, her favorite dish), and doing homework. After all, she has nine years until high school graduation.

Srivastav Family

“Here, at least, we can give the children food.”

— Moolchand Srivastav

Varsha Srivastav

11 years old
Time children spend home alone: 9 hours per day
Distance traveled: 277 miles
Started: Fatehabad, Haryana
Ended: Agra, Uttar Pradesh

As Varsha Srivastav and her younger brother, Aditya, walk home after school, they know what they’re coming home to: an almost empty house. They’re the equivalent of latchkey kids — both parents are away at work during the day, so the children are on their own until about 5:30 p.m.

The 11-year-old girl and 7-year-old boy make their way from the school, which sits on the eastern bank of the Yamuna River, across from the Taj Mahal in Agra. They pass houses, temples, busy streets, and, just before reaching their house, a pond of standing water covered in trash.

Their older sister, Pooja, 15, will be home when Varsha and Aditya return from school. She was pulled out of school several years ago to care for a sick aunt, and she’s never gone back. She’s alone at home all day.

Being home alone in Shambu Nagar, the neighborhood where their family lives in Agra, isn’t safe. With its blocks of crumbling brick homes just big enough for sleeping and cooking, most of life is lived in the open, including bathing, playing, and studying.

The Taj Mahal sits across the river from Varsha’s neighborhood in Agra.

Alone at home without a parent, the siblings are easy prey for trafficking, child labor, and assault.

“While I’m working, I’m always worried about my children,” says their mother, Anita. “I have a girl who is an adolescent and people know that she’s home alone. Who knows what they’re thinking.”

With their home situated right off the neighborhood’s main thoroughfare, the family’s daily activities like washing dishes, brushing teeth, and even bathing are done in plain sight of their neighbors.
Varsha and Pooja fix their hair to get ready for the day – one leaves for school, and the other stays home to cook and do chores.
Using a paste made from powder and water, Varsha and Aditya brush their teeth next to drying clothes.
Inside their one-room home, Anita prepares breakfast and tea for her husband and children.
Pooja walks past the pond of standing water and garbage located right next to her family’s home.

Anita, 40, used to work as a housemaid for a wealthy family, but she lost that job and now works in a factory that makes grommets for tents. She leaves home at 8:30 a.m. to walk to work. The journey takes her across a bridge that spans the Yamuna, heavy with foot traffic and train tracks. The bridge, on one side, offers a perfect view of the Taj Mahal’s white marble domes.

Married at 14 years old, Anita’s life has revolved around caring for her family 26 years.
Pooja, alone at home during the day, makes flatbread to eat with dinner.

Anita’s wracked with guilt about leaving her children alone at home, yet she has no other choice: Her husband, Moolchand, works pulling a rickshaw and doesn’t earn enough to sustain the family of six. He makes 6,000 rupees a month (US$92), but he rents the rickshaw for 50 rupees a day (US 75 cents).

Before she walks to work in the morning, Anita packs lunch — usually lentils and rice — for herself and Moolchand into metal tiffin carriers. Lunch in hand, she sets off at 8:30 a.m. to her job at a factory that makes grommets for tents. As she leaves the neighborhood, Anita passes houses freshly painted for the upcoming Diwali festival. She crosses a bridge that serves pedestrians and trains on her 30-minute walk. The bridge offers a view of the Taj Mahal’s white domes.

As difficult as it is to make ends meet, the family’s situation was worse before they migrated to Agra 15 years ago. Anita and Moolchand, 50, came from a district in northwestern Haryana state called Fatehabad seeking better job opportunities.

In Fatehabad, Moolchand was a nut roaster, and the work was based on a barter system — there was no exchange of money. At one point, he says, the family went eight days without food. They had no toilets or electricity.

“Here, at least, we can give the children food,” Moolchand says.

Financial difficulty drove their decision to marry off their two oldest daughters, Preeti and Anjana, as young teenagers. Anita was also married at 14 because of her family’s hardship, and she had her first child two years later.

“I want my children to be more wise and informed, so they are able to live a good quality of life,” says Anita.

With two teenage daughters already married, it’s likely the same fate awaits Pooja, despite Anita’s wish. The family’s poverty, Pooja’s lack of education, and cultural tradition form a combination ripe for child marriage.

Inside Varsha’s (top center) open air classroom at school, the teacher quizzes her students on vocabulary words. Outside the school walls, children who work making shoes hang around before classes begin.

With Varsha and Aditya in school, there’s more possibility for a different life from their older sisters. But their attendance can be sporadic, and Aditya often begs his mother to let him stay home. Today, she gently chided him to go; his unhappiness made it clear he often gets his way.

In the morning, Anita makes tea for the family and prepares lunch for everyone to take with them to school and work — today it’s lentils, rice, potatoes, and cauliflower. Later in the day, the children cook dinner for themselves on the small silver propane stove that sits on the floor of the family’s one-room home.

Home by themselves for several hours until Anita returns from work, Varsha and Pooja get started on making dinner for the family inside their one-room home.

Unlike other families in Shambu Nagar, the family keeps to themselves — it’s just the six of them. There aren’t friends, neighbors, or extended family stopping by or checking in on the children at home.

Another threat to their safety: The twice-daily trek they used to make to a field of tall grass to use the bathroom. The girls feared men and snakes hiding in the grass — both real possibilities — until World Vision built the family a toilet two years ago.

The overgrown field where Pooja and her family members once had to use as a bathroom is just off a busy street. The sisters were scared to walk there alone, fearing snakes in the tall grass and harassment from men.
The family now has a toilet right outside their door, constructed by World Vision two years ago. Now, says Anita, “We’re not publicly declaring to everyone that you’re using the toilet. We feel like we have privacy.”

“We feel safer now,” says Anita. “You’re not publicly declaring to everyone that you’re using the toilet. Now we feel like we have privacy.”

She faces a difficult decision every morning: Stay at home, ensuring her children are safe, or walk to the factory and work long hours in hope of lifting the family out of poverty, leaving the children unsupervised. Both options have short- and long-term consequences.

The stretch of the Yamuna River where Varsha and Aditya’s school is located stays busy all day with people washing clothes — and some openly defecating at the water’s edge.

Moolchand and Anita made a similarly difficult decision to leave their rural village and move to Agra. The trade-off solved one problem — food — but introduced the issue of safety and security for their children. It’s a choice they likely would make again for their family’s basic survival.

Singh Family

“Her sense of confidence was lacking, but now she is more bold.”

— Vimlesh Singh

Varsha Singh

12 years old
Time until fulfilling her dream of working at a bank: 10 years
Distance traveled: 236 miles
Started: Sultanpur, Uttarakhand
Ended: Agra, Uttar Pradesh

About 25 yards from where Varsha Singh sits poised with a paintbrush in her hand, a train rolls by, shaking her family’s home. The commotion doesn’t bother Varsha, 12. She dips the brush into bright vermillion paint and brings to life a colorful pattern on a simple clay pot — one of many ways her handmade decorations make an otherwise plain house beautiful.

For Varsha, art imitates life. Her entire family are agents of change in their neighborhood, Raj Nagar, a section of Agra where poverty is widespread and the main industry, other than tourism related to the Taj Mahal, is shoemaking. Children often drop out of school and work to help support their families, many before they learn to read or write.

The family isn’t without their own struggles. But despite financial hardships, her parents, Jawahar and Vimlesh, take advantage of every available opportunity to better their kids’ lives — starting with the decision to migrate to Agra 25 years ago.

Older brother Yogesh sits just yards from the family’s home where trains rattle by every 15 minutes.
Varsha’s delicate brushstrokes decorate a clay candle holder for the upcoming Diwali festival.

Originally from Sultanpur, a small city in the far eastern part of Uttar Pradesh state, Jawahar, 46, was a day laborer. In Agra, he makes shoes, but it’s not steady work. He hasn’t had a regular job in three months, and he takes out loans when his earnings — on average, 6,500 rupees a month (US$99) — aren’t enough make ends meet.

He was 24 when he married Vimlesh, who was 14.

In one corner of the family’s high-ceilinged bedroom, Vimlesh cooks flatbread dotted with chopped onion on a small propane stove. The shelf liners, crafted from newspaper, are another of Varsha’s artistic touches around the home.

“I was petrified” at the thought of marriage, Vimlesh says. She was in eighth grade at the time and dropped out of school to run her new household. In India, 18 percent of girls are married before they turn 15.

“I didn’t understand what marriage was, and we fought a lot,” she says. “I have made a pact with myself that I won’t marry off my girls before age 20. They need to be more wise about life.”

For Varsha and her sister, Ashu, 14, life follows a different path than their mother’s.

Varsha is one of 25 Smart Children in her neighborhood, tasked with regularly checking in on other sponsored children and monitoring for signs of abuse and malnutrition. “I volunteered to be a Smart Child because I want to bring change to my community,” says Varsha.
The program gives Varsha and Ashul a chance to develop leadership skills and become active participants in community life, and it also helps ensure the wellbeing of all children in the neighborhood.
The group of 25 Smart Children meets regularly to discuss problems and find solutions together.
“I felt very good that they get to go out and do productive work” as Smart Children, says Varsha’s mother, Vimlesh. “It’s good that the kids are involved in the changes and development of the community.”

The sisters, less concerned with running a household, focus instead on keeping up with their studies — Varsha excels in both art and math. Extracurriculars include a children’s club where they learn about and advocate for children’s rights and a child monitoring program called Smart Child.

The Smart Child program trains teenagers — chosen by local World Vision staff — to ensure sponsored children are in school, look for signs of abuse and child labor, and establish trust among neighbors.

Through this, Varsha and the other Smart Children, including her sister, learn how to interact with adults, develop leadership skills, and increase their confidence.

“Before, I kept to my family,” says Varsha. “But I’ve been more outgoing and created a sense of responsibility. I care about these issues.”

Her mother agrees: “Her sense of confidence was lacking, but now she is more bold,” says Vimlesh. “Her leadership qualities are being developed.”

The same can be said of Vimlesh. Since she’s become involved in changing her community, especially through a savings group where she’s been elected president, “I feel more open, I feel more comfortable talking to people,” she says. “Now whatever I’m thinking, I’m able to say.”

Varsha helps out with chores around the house in between school, her duties as a Smart Child, and participation in the neighborhood’s children’s club.

In the courtyard area of her family’s home, Ashul braids her hair before she and Varsha walk to school. They attend a public school, while younger brother Nikhil goes to private school.

She cringes thinking of how meek she used to be. “Don’t remind me of how I was when I was young!” she says, laughing. “I used to cover my head and face. I wouldn’t make eye contact, I was shy and submissive, and I was embarrassed to go outside the house.”

About This Project

Urbanization has changed the face of poverty over the last century, which means it’s changed the way World Vision works in cities.

In 2008, we launched the Urban Research Initiative to build on our decades of expertise in sustainable community development in rural areas — to see what works in an urban setting, what doesn’t, and how to replicate learnings in cities around the world.

For this project, we wanted to explore what compels people to uproot their lives in pursuit of a better future for their children and if their new life delivers on that promise.

We were also interested to see how World Vision’s work manifests in urban areas: How does our community development model flex to address specific challenges in specific contexts? We went to two cities in India, Agra and Delhi, to find out.

Some things are starkly different, like water — we can’t drill boreholes in dense city neighborhoods, but we can provide purifiers for the water the city pipes in — and some of it is strikingly similar. People everywhere are remarkably resourceful and tenacious in solving problems and overcoming challenges. Child sponsorship functions the same way in urban and rural environments, too. A child with the knowledge that someone believes in them, and the resulting empowerment, transforms communities one family at a time.

In Delhi, one of the largest cities in the world, it’s easy to feel anonymous, hopeless, and insignificant. The systematic problems are so complex that they seem immovable and unconquerable. But World Vision chips away at these challenges by creating change in the smallest denominator possible: a child.

Despite the challenges of urban poverty, this approach of individual transformation works — and is changing lives.

Agra

Agra, a city of almost 1.6 million people in northern India, owes its modern existence to the Taj Mahal, though the area has been populated since the 14th century. It was India's capital city twice, and reminders of past rulers — from the Mughals and Marathas to the British Raj — dot the city.

Built on the banks of the Yamuna River, Agra is a global tourist destination. But beyond the Taj Mahal’s white marble domes and Agra Fort’s red walls lies a sprawling city with pockets of poverty.

World Vision has been working in neighborhoods across the city since 2013, and hundreds of children have sponsors in the U.S. Staff in Agra partner with community leaders to address problems of child labor and abuse, enroll children in school, fight cultural traditions like child marriage and a preference for sons over daughters, and construct toilets where open defecation is common.

Corners of the city vary drastically. Wealthy neighborhoods sit close to New Delhi’s government center, with high-end restaurants, boutiques, and parks sequestered in leafy enclaves. Cyber City is a collection of modern steel-and-glass buildings filled with offices of companies like Microsoft, KPMG, and Google. Slums and resettlement colonies house millions of urban poor across the city.

World Vision’s work in Delhi is split into several areas, and more than 5,000 children are sponsored across the city. In recent years, programs have focused on child protection and building resilience, adding to past successful work in education; water, sanitation, and hygiene; economic empowerment; and health.

Pollution in Agra

Pollution in Agra harms the city’s air, land, and water. People in poor neighborhoods close to the Taj Mahal often burn trash for warmth, discoloring the monument’s white marble. Visitors rarely see the building without scaffolding, erected to clean its façade.

Lack of municipal services, like sewers and trash collection, are the biggest factors in Agra’s pollution problems. Open defecation is still common — drainage canals in slum areas are clogged with trash and waste, and the Yamuna River’s banks and water are a mess of litter and human and animal waste.

Child Marriage

One in three child brides in the world lives in India, according to UNICEF. Across the country, an estimated 47 percent of girls under 18 are married — the highest number of child brides in the world. Child marriage remains a widespread custom that transcends all levels of society.

Pollution in Delhi

In November 2016, Delhi’s shroud of smog made international headlines. Already the planet’s most polluted city, a combination of Diwali fireworks, traffic, farmers outside the city burning fields after the harvest, and coal plants broke the capital’s existing records. Government officials declared an emergency, and schools across the city were forced to close. The haze, which health experts liken to smoking two packs of cigarettes per day, usually dissipates in three or four days — but this time it lingered more than a week.

Air pollution is one of the only equalizers in a city with a massive gulf between rich and poor. The wealthy suffer along with the impoverished, but trash burning for warmth in poor neighborhoods makes a bad problem worse.

Economic Empowerment

Economic empowerment is a core piece of World Vision’s community development work around the world in both rural and urban areas. While sponsorship looks basically the same in most contexts, economic development assistance varies widely, says Jamila, a staff member in Agra who has worked in both rural and urban World Vision projects. In rural areas, giving animals and seeds to families, along with training, works well to boost their livelihoods in sustainable ways. But in urban areas, she says, it’s about initial investment in a business — like the pushcart and bangles Kashish’s mother, Solenka Kumar, received or the sewing machines Tipu’s parents were given.

Gender Preference

India, like many countries around the world, has long-held traditional expectations around girls and boys, many of which give preferential treatment to boys — sometimes before a child is born. On average in India, for every 100 girls born, 110 boys are born. Gender preference has far-reaching implications: child marriage for young girls, prenatal sex selection, infanticide, violence against girls and women, and even giving boys more (or more nutritious) food than girls. Many couples continue having children until they have a son, even at the cost of the mother’s health.

Harassment

In 2012, the brutal rape and subsequent death of a female medical student on a Delhi bus made headlines across the globe. Similar incidents continue to come to light, as Delhi ranks as the most unsafe city in India. In just one year, from 2014 to 2015, crime against women here grew 20 percent.

Often called “teasing,” a term that understates the extent of the problem, even normal exchanges between boys and girls can quickly escalate. A 2012 U.N. study found that 92 percent of women in Delhi reported experiencing some sort of public sexual violence in their lifetime. High unemployment and school dropout rates mean there are usually men and teenage boys hanging around with little to do, and India’s patriarchal culture often gives them the freedom to act with nothing to lose.

To combat the problem, World Vision partnered with Delhi police to train 250 teenage girls in self-defense techniques, including Tipu’s sister, Heena, and Arti.

Kids Not in School

Almost 12 million children ages 6 to 13 in India don’t attend school.

Source: U.N.

Malnutrition

About 60 million children in India — nearly half of all the country’s children — are underweight. India’s rate of malnutrition in children are twice those in sub-Saharan Africa.

Two major contributing factors: teenage mothers who aren’t healthy enough themselves, and lack of knowledge about proper feeding and care for babies and young children.

Source: World Bank

Diwali

One of the most popular Hindu festivals throughout the year, Diwali is celebrated every autumn, usually between mid-October and mid-November. People clean their homes — and sometimes renovate or paint the exterior — string up colored lights, and purchase new clothes and gifts in the days leading up to the festival. Because Diwali signifies the triumph of light over darkness, traditionally candles or lamps are placed around homes. Fireworks are another part of the celebration, contributing to air pollution in major cities.

Shoemaking

Second only to tourism in Agra, shoemaking has been big business since the Mughal era. There was even a documentary made about the industry: 2015’s “In Their Shoes.” Carts piled high with shoeboxes crowd the streets, factories across the city employ thousands, and a glimpse inside many homes reveals parents — and sometimes children — constructing shoes. Children often drop out of school to work making shoes for extra income.

Kashish’s father, Vinod Kumar, works in two different factories. He’s on his feet 12 hours a day at his workstation making shoes from start to finish: cutting pieces from a template, stacking them neatly, and finally stitching them all together. Preeti’s father, Om Sholanki, and others who make leather whips purchase leather scraps from the factories to make the whips they sell.

Trains

A legacy of British rule over the Indian subcontinent, a vast network of trains crisscrosses India connecting points north, south, east, and west. Each year, the railway system moves an estimated 8.107 billion passengers through more than 8,000 train stations across the country.

India’s railways facilitate migration, making it easy for masses of people to move from place to place. Eight class types reflect the country’s traditional hierarchy. Luxe, air-conditioned trains transport affluent Indians and many tourists, while the majority of the population travels on the cheapest trains — designated with light blue cars, hard wooden seats, and often unbearable overcrowding.

Uber

Uber, the rideshare app that’s taken the taxi industry by storm, has expanded across India, with an estimated 240,000 drivers in 29 cities. In Delhi, riders can choose from six Uber options, including Ubermoto — just hop on the back of the driver’s motorbike — to get around the capital. It’s even possible to take an Uber from affluent neighborhoods to resettlement colonies like Madanpur Khadar. Though India is Uber’s biggest Asian market, it has stiff competition with Ola, a local ridesharing company.

Anganwadi

Anganwadis are government-run centers for mothers and children across India. Most rural villages have an anganwadi, and in Agra, there’s one in many of the neighborhoods where World Vision works. They provide basic pre-natal nutrition and care to pregnant mothers, teach new moms proper nutrition for themselves and their babies, and often hold informal preschool-like classes for young children.

Because the network of centers already exists and functions well with workers paid by the government, World Vision supports anganwadis in its program areas with educational materials, training for teachers, and other supplies like water purifiers.

Magic Bus

Magic Bus, the nonprofit organization where Tipu works, employs 16 staff in their South Delhi program, which reaches 10,000 children. Magic Bus works in cities and villages across India, teaching children about respecting themselves and their parents, health, education, sports, and social and emotional learning.

Multinational Corporations

Foreign investment in recent decades brought multinational corporations like Microsoft, Deloitte, Philips, DuPont, Google, KPMG, and other Fortune 500 companies to India — most with offices just outside Delhi city limits in a collection of buildings known as Cyber City.

Savings Groups

In many areas where World Vision works — both rural and urban — savings groups teach the concept and importance of saving money. They also empower women in the community, often mothers of sponsored children, to develop financial literacy and leadership skills.

Vimlesh Singh, Varsha’s mother, is president of her neighborhood’s savings group. The 13 members meet monthly, and each contributes 100 rupees (US$1.55) to the group fund, which is then deposited at a local bank. Members can take out loans at 1 percent interest for health emergencies, school tuition, or even capital to start a business.

Preeti’s mother and several aunts and cousins are members of Jeevan Jyoti savings group, which means “life light.” The name comes from their desire to “help each other and brighten our community,” says group leader Leela. The 14 members give 50 rupees (US 80 cents) each at their monthly meetings and can take out loans at 2 percent interest. “Now with minimum interest we are able to help people who need it,” says Leela. “Apart from financial help, we find a source and place to talk. We become voices for our community.”

Smart Child

World Vision’s Smart Child program accomplishes two things at once: develop responsibility and leadership skills in older children and teenagers and monitor neighborhoods for signs of harm to children — abuse, exploitation, child marriage, trafficking, and working instead of attending school.

Though the concept came from World Vision staff, children like Varsha and Ashu volunteered to become a Smart Child. They’re each responsible for covering one block in their neighborhood, going door to door to homes where sponsored children live and keeping an eye out for other children. At monthly meetings, they’re trained on children’s rights, how to spot problems, and ways to report their concerns.

“I volunteered to be a Smart Child because I want to bring change to my community. We get insight into the challenges our community is facing, like the kids who aren’t going to school. Before, I kept to my family. But I’ve been more outgoing and created a sense of responsibility, and I care about these issues. We get to talk about our opinions. There are four kinds of rights — protection, participation, life, and development. In my community, children don’t always get these four rights.” — Varsha Singh, 12

“The Smart Children have become like role models. Other parents want their kids to become like them. Varsha and Ashul were chosen because they are very active girls. Most people on the street know them.” — Jamila, World Vision staff member of nine years

“It’s good that the kids are involved in the changes and development of the community. It instills in them to be aware of problems affecting children. They became more accountable and responsible for the community.” — Vimlesh Singh, Varsha and Ashu’s mother

Lodi Gardens

Delhi’s equivalent to New York’s Central Park, Lodi Gardens is an oasis of green amid a bustling city. Its 90 acres contain lush lawns, walking paths, and architecturally significant tombs of rulers dating back to the 1400s.

Lodi Gardens is a favorite spot among upper-class Delhi residents and diplomats, many of whom live in the surrounding neighborhood. Families bring picnics and play games of badminton to a backdrop of birdsongs. It’s also a must-see on many tourist itineraries.

Nehru Place

Now a thriving business district, Nehru Place was once a sprawling slum. The area is named after India’s first prime minister, Jawaharlal Nehru. Because residents didn’t own the land where they lived when the government decided to develop the area, thousands of families were relocated to the outskirts of Delhi in 2002. Their new neighborhoods, including Madanpur Khadar, initially lacked basic amenities like water, sanitation facilities, and schools.

World Vision’s programs in South Delhi originally centered around Nehru Place, but when families there were resettled in Madanpur Khadar, World Vision followed.

Taj Mahal

One of the most recognizable buildings in the world, the Taj Mahal sits less than a mile from neighborhoods where World Vision works in Agra. Mughal emperor Shah Jahan built the Taj Mahal in the 1600s as a mausoleum for his wife Mumtaz Mahal. Because of its architectural and cultural significance, it was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983.

The gleaming white marble monument and surrounding gardens attract 7 to 8 million visitors every year. Indians pay 510 rupees (US$8) to enter, which means that many who can see the white domes from their own neighborhood — like Preeti, who dreams of visiting the Taj Mahal — can’t afford to go inside. The entrance fee for foreign visitors is 750 rupees (US$11.60).

Yamuna River

A major tributary of the Ganges, the Yamuna River is sacred to Hindus. The 855-mile-long river stretches from the Himalayas — where it starts as clear glacier melt — to Allahabad, where it meets the Ganges full of sewage, trash, and industrial waste. On its journey, it passes through both Agra and Delhi. Pollution threatens the slow-moving Yamuna, especially around the capital, where many of the urban poor live on or near its banks.
