Open Air Amenities, More Crucial in Property Development in the New Normal

November 10, 2020

Table of Contents

  • Why Open Air Amenities Matter
  • How Amenities Promote Health and Safety
  • Where Amenity Innovations Are Headed

Open air amenities should be a priority in development plans, as the real estate sector recovers from the pandemic. This is one of the important insights thought leaders concluded in the recently held virtual Lamudi roundtable discussion, New Behaviors, New Demands: What Matters to Homebuyers Now.

With social distancing rules and the emphasis on proper ventilation during this time, open-air facilities have been more popular among property buyers. In Provincial cities and overseas interest contribute to real estate resilience, the latest trend report Lamudi released, balconies, gardens, and fully fenced spaces were the top-searched home features. 

They represented 12.34 percent of searches, pointing to people’s need for a home that promotes well-being, the report said.

According to several executives who graced the online discussion, open-air amenities, as important as they are to residents’ lifestyle of wellness, have already been a part of their projects even before the pandemic.

Why Open Air Amenities Matter

Jenna Belardo, AVP and Business Unit Head for Property Sales of Ortigas Land, talked about a township the company planned back in 2006, which featured areas that provide recreational opportunities for residents.

“The estate has a lot of open spaces, open-air activities. It even has a basketball court. It has a lot of gardens. It’s all accessible from a podium, from a residential building.

“Inside the residential building, there are sky gardens. All units have balconies,” she said. 

The rationale behind such amenities is to incorporate nature in urban living, according to Belardo.

“We know how condo dwelling is. It’s not like a subdivision or horizontal development that you have your backyards or front yards,” she pointed out. They wanted to show that it’s possible to enjoy these leisure facilities even in vertical living.

Although they weren’t preparing for a pandemic, Belardo noted that such residential amenities became useful amid the current crisis.

She said that their residential project in Capitol Commons catered best to plantitos and plantitas during this time, as the units come with balconies.

“[Our residents] really appreciate the balconies because they have a place for their plants,” she mentioned.

How Amenities Promote Health and Safety

For DataLand, open-air amenities have likewise always been part of their developments. Felimon Yee, Jr., Vice President for Business Development and Customer Turnover, said they designate jogging paths in their projects to give residents more open space.

Easing some restrictions on amenities, they started to open up meditation areas, sky lounges, game rooms, and study areas. He emphasized, however, that they’re doing it with caution, prioritizing the community’s health and safety.

“[Dwellers should] have a schedule and observe protocols, [such as] social distancing,” he said.

Harping also on the well-being of residents, Elizabeth Ventura, President of Anchor Land Holdings, Inc., shared that while open-air amenities have been in place in their projects, they recently introduced extra health safety features, such as air cleaning innovations.

She said, “Yes, open space is considered every time in every project, yet, adding to that is the purification systems that we have in our units.”

Where Amenity Innovations Are Headed

Markus Nisula, Managing Director of KONE Philippines, said that certain technologies are now available for filtering air even in enclosed, small spaces, including elevators. It’s a welcome development, considering the reports that certain viruses can remain suspended in the air for a period of time.

Noting that clean air is extremely important inside such facilities given the close proximity of passengers to each other, he said, “[KONE] has a solution where we have a UV light on the top of the elevator car, and then the air goes around the filters and this UV light disinfection system.”

Aside from elevator air purifiers, Nisula shared their company’s predictive maintenance technologies. He explained how they work: “The elevators are actually sending signals about their condition every other second to cloud. Then, there’s an artificial intelligence system analyzing that data. 

“If they see something peculiar, like for example, the doors don’t close properly, then we can send our technician and fix the problem before the elevator breaks down.”

Nisula said that these innovations increase the sense of security of residents. He believes this is crucial in bringing back the confidence of the market amid these trying times.

“Beneath the surface, there’s still uncertainty. We need joint effort to find those innovations that can build trust with homeowners, so they’re ready to invest in developments, which are future-proof.”
