Key Findings

Federal & coverage snapshot: the FMLA provides unpaid leave for serious illness but there is no universal federal paid sick‑leave requirement. Private‑sector access rose from 63% to 77% (2010–2023), largely driven by state/local mandates; about 20 states plus Washington, D.C. now require paid sick leave while 18 states preempt local ordinances. Many low‑wage and food/hospitality workers remain uncovered and public support for paid sick days is strong.Common law features to reflect in an employee handbook template: accrual formulas (e.g., 1 hour per 30/34/35/40/52 hours or fractional rates such as Nevada’s 0.01923 hours per hour for employers of 50+), employer‑size thresholds and tiered requirements, annual caps (commonly 40, 48, 56, 64 hours), carryover rules, waiting periods (commonly 90 or 120 days), and "safe leave" provisions; local ordinances (e.g., NYC, Philadelphia, Portland, Montgomery County) can be more generous and must be checked for city/county‑level compliance.Recent/forthcoming state specifics employers should watch when buying or drafting a template: Nebraska’s law (effective Oct 2025) — accrual 1 hour per 30, 40–56 hours depending on employer size; Virginia (law passed Mar 2026) — 1 hour per 30 up to 40 hours plus a separate payroll‑funded 12‑week family/medical program; other representative examples: Minnesota (1/30 up to 48 hours), New Jersey (1/30 up to 40 hours, usable after 120 days), New York (1/30 with 40–56 hour caps by employer size), Oregon (1/40 for employers of 10+ up to 40 hours, usable after 90 days), Washington State (1/40, unlimited accrual but 40‑hour carryover), Washington, D.C. (tiered required days by employer size: 3/5/7 days).

Summary

Federal context: the Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) provides unpaid leave for serious illness but the United States has no universal paid sick‑leave guarantee; federal employees receive 13 paid sick days/year and federal proposals (e.g., the Healthy Families Act) have been discussed. Public opinion strongly favors paid sick days.Coverage and trends: private‑sector access rose from 63% to 77% (2010–2023), largely due to state and local mandates; as of 2025, 20 states plus Washington, D.C. require paid sick leave while 18 states preempt local ordinances—yet many workers, especially low‑wage and food/hospitality workers, remain without paid sick time.Common policy features and examples: laws typically specify accrual rates, employer‑size thresholds, caps, waiting periods, carryover rules, and “safe leave” for victims of violence. Examples: Minnesota — 1 hour per 30 worked, up to 48 hours/year; Nevada — 0.01923 hours per hour for employers of 50+ (usable after 90 days); New Jersey — 1 hour per 30, up to 40 hours, usable after 120 days; New York — 1 hour per 30 with 40–56 hour caps depending on employer size (localities such as NYC may be more generous).Additional state specifics: Nebraska (new law effective Oct 2025) — accrual 1 hour per 30 with 40–56 hours depending on employer size; Oregon — employers of 10+ must provide up to 40 hours, accrual 1 hour per 40 worked, usable after 90 days; Rhode Island — employers with >18 employees up to 40 hours, accrual 1 per 34 with differing waiting periods; Vermont — employees working >18 hours/week entitled up to 40 hours, accrual 1 per 52; Virginia (law passed March 2026) — 1 hour per 30 up to 40 hours plus a separate payroll‑funded 12‑week family/medical program; Washington — 1 per 40 hours, unlimited accrual but 40 hours carryover; Washington, D.C. — tiered required days by employer size (3/5/7 days).

TheUnited Statesfederal governmentrequiresunpaid leavefor serious illnesses, but does not require that employees have access to paidsick leaveto address their own short-term illnesses or the short-term illness of a family member. The United States is one of few high-income countries not to guarantee universal paid sick leave.[1]

The share of private sector jobs with paid sick leave policies has increased from 63 to 77 percent over the period 2010 to 2023; mostly as a result of state-level requirements to provide sick leave.[1]A number of states and localities do require some or all employers to provide paid sick leave to their workers. Some jurisdictions allow for "safe leave", the use of sick leave for health and safety reasons related to domestic violence, sexual assault, stalking, and other forms of violence and harassment.

Availability

A 2009 analysis from theBureau of Labor Statistics(BLS) found that around 39 percent of American workers in the private sector do not have paid sick leave.[2]Around 79 percent of workers in low-wage industries do not have paid sick time.[3]Most food service and hotel workers (78 percent) lack paid sick days.[4]

A 2008 survey reported that 77 percent of Americans believe that having paid sick days is "very important" for workers.[5]Some workers report that they or a family member have been fired or suspended for missing work due to illness.[6]

A 2020 paper found that requiring paid sick leave in the US likely increased overallwell-being.[7]When paid sick leave is required by law, workers tended to take two more days off work each year.[7]

Unpaid sick leave

The US requires unpaid leave for serious illnesses through theFamily and Medical Leave Act(FMLA). This law requires most medium-sized and larger employers to comply and, within those businesses, covers employees who have worked for their employer for at least 12 months prior to taking the leave.[8]

Paid sick leave

As of 2025, 20 states andWashington DCrequire paid sick leave, while 30 states do not. 18 states preempt local governments from requiring paid sick leave. Some localities require paid sick leave.[9][10][11]

Alabama

Alabama does not require paid sick leave and preempts local governments from requiring it.[9][10]

Alaska

All employees earn one hour of paid sick leave for every 30 hours worked. Employees of companies with 15 or more employees can accrue up to 56 hours a year, while employees of smaller companies can only accrue 40 hours a year.[22]

Arizona

All full-time, part-time, and temporary workers earn one hour of paid sick leave for every 30 hours worked. Employees of companies with 15 or more employees can accrue up to 40 hours a year, while employees of smaller companies can only accrue 24 hours a year. Companies are only required to allow employees to use their time off after being employed for 90 days.[9]

Arkansas

California

Companies are only required to allow employees to use their time off after being employed for 90 days.San Francisco,Oakland,Long Beach,San Diego,Emeryville,Santa Monica,Los Angeles, andBerkeleyhave more expansive requirements.[9][24]

Colorado

All employees earn one hour of paid sick leave for every 30 hours worked and can accrue and use up to 48 a year, with unused hours being carried over. If a public health emergency is declared, full-time workers are allowed an additional 80 hours off, while part-time workers are allowed an additional number of days equal to their average days worked a week or schedules to work in 14 days.[9]

Connecticut

Companies with 50 or more employees are required to provide paid sick leave to hourly, service workers. Temporary or day workers are not included. Employees earn one hour off for every 40 hours worked and can use up to 40 hours a year, with unused hours carrying over.[9]

Maryland

Companies with 25 or more employees are required to give anyone who works over 12 hours a week paid sick and safe leave. Proposed policies in the United States

Public opinion

In August 2008, theNational Opinion Research Centerat theUniversity of Chicagoreleased their findings from a national public opinion poll on paid sick days.[33]

86 percent of people surveyed said they favor a basic paid sick day policy.94 percent of self-identified liberals and 81 percent of self-identified conservatives believed that paid sick day should be a basic workplace right.77 percent of respondents believed that paid sick days were very important.63 percent of workers who do not have access to paid sick leave said they are concerned about not having paid sick days.16 percent of workers report that they or a family member have been fired, suspended, or otherwise punished or that they would be fired if they missed work due to illness.46 percent of respondents said they are more likely to vote for a candidate who supports paid sick days.

U.S. federal legislation

Since 2004, theHealthy Families Act(HR 2460 / S 1152) has repeatedly been proposed in Congress.[1]It would establish a basic workplace mandate of paid sick days so workers can take paid sick days to care for their health or the health of their families.

The bill creates a minimum requirement that allows workers to earn up to seven days per year of paid leave to recover from illness, to care for a sick family member, or to seek preventative health care. It enables victims of domestic violence, stalking, and sexual assault to take paid time off to recover from incidents and seek assistance from the police or court. It also allows people to take time off to care for ill parents and elderly relatives, or to attend diagnostic or routine medical appointments. Employers with fewer than 15 workers would be exempt from the law.

The Healthy Families Act would allow an additional 30 million workers to have access to paid sick leave from their jobs, including 15 million low-wage workers and 13 million women workers. If the bill were to become law, 90 percent of all American workers would have access to paid sick days (up from 61 percent currently).[34]

A version of the bill was first introduced in 2004. Each session, it has gained support inside and outside of Congress.[citation needed]CongresswomanRosa DeLauroand SenatorEdward Kennedyreintroduced the Healthy Families Act in the 111th Congress in May 2009.[35]After Senator Kennedy's death, SenatorChris Doddbecame the lead Senate sponsor of the Healthy Families Act. The bill currently has 125 co-sponsors in the House and 24 in the Senate.

The Healthy Families Act was the subject of three hearings in the111th United States Congress:

House Education and Labor Committee's Workforce Protections Subcommittee hearing on theHealthy Families Acton June 11, 2009.Senate Committee on Health, Education, Labor and Pensions, Subcommittee on Children and Families hearing,"The Cost of Being Sick: H1N1 and Paid Sick Days,"on November 10, 2009.House Education and Labor Committee hearing,"Protecting Employees, Employers and the Public: H1N1 and Sick Leave Policies,"on November 17, 2009.

TheObama Administrationhas testified in support of the bill.[36]First Lady Michelle Obama has spoken out on numerous occasions about the need for a paid sick day mandate.[37]

The U.S. government guarantees federal employees 13 paid sick days a year.[38]
