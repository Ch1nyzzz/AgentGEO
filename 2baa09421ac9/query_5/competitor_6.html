Abstract

Many medieval manuscripts are illuminated with paintings and other graphic elements, one purpose of which may have been to reinforce the significance of the work in question with a pictorial gloss, and perhaps also as a visual aid to convey its message for the benefit of readers who may not always have been literate. Reading the text through pictures is a matter of particular interest in the case of historical literature, as chronicles often commissioned at court lend themselves especially well to a deliberate programme of enhancing the image of the ruler and celebrating his deeds according to the political concepts and ideological imperatives of the time. This paper addresses the question of the illustration of historical texts within the Persian tradition of book art, focusing on the Jami' al-tawarikh of Rashid al-Din and its impact on later productions.

FAQ's

What drives the selective illustration of Persian historical manuscripts?add

The paper reveals that artistic patronage and audience dynamics significantly influence which chronicles are illustrated, particularly highlighting the selective illustrations during the Mongol period.

How did the 'Jami' al-tawarikh' influence later Persian historiography?add

Rashid al-Din's 'Jami' al-tawarikh' inspired a tradition of illustrated histories, heavily referenced by later historians like Hafiz-i Abru and Mirkhwand, promoting a narrative integration of Persian identity and Mongol rule.

What relationship exists between text and image in Persian historical manuscripts?add

The study finds that the relationship is often non-linear, with illustrations complementing narratives rather than directly depicting text, as illustrated by the artistic omissions regarding significant battles in the 'Jami' al-tawarikh'.

When did Persian manuscript illustration peak and what influenced it?add

Illustration peaked from the early 14th to mid-16th centuries, driven by royal patronage, exemplified by the flourishing illustrated works during the Timurid and Mughal periods.

What unique characteristics define illustrated Persian chronicles compared to other traditions?add

The research indicates that Persian chronicles uniquely blend historical narrative with epic storytelling, evidenced by illustrations that draw heavily on mythic pasts, as seen in depictions from the 'Shahnama'.
