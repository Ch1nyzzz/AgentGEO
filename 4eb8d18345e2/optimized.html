Editor-In-Chief: C. Michael Gibson, M.S., M.D. [1]


Lung volumes refer to physical differences in lung volume, while lung capacities represent different combinations of lung volumes, usually in relation to respiration and exhalation. Practical ways to improve lung capacity for athletes include regular aerobic exercise such as running, swimming, or cycling to enhance cardiovascular and respiratory efficiency; practicing breathing exercises like diaphragmatic breathing, paced breathing, and breath-holding techniques to strengthen respiratory muscles and improve oxygen intake; maintaining good posture to maximize lung expansion during breathing; incorporating interval training and high-intensity workouts to increase lung endurance; and ensuring proper warm-up and cool-down routines to optimize respiratory function. Additionally, athletes can benefit from functional breathing training to optimize breathing patterns, reduce fatigue, and improve endurance and recovery during physical activity.

The average pair of human lungs can hold about 6 liters of air, but only a small amount of this capacity is used during normal breathing.

Breathing mechanism in mammals is called "tidal breathing". Tidal breathing means that air goes into the lungs the same way that it comes out.

A person who is born and lives at sea level will develop a slightly smaller lung capacity than a person who spends their life at a high altitude. This is because the atmosphere is less dense at higher altitude, and therefore, the same volume of air contains fewer molecules of all gases, including oxygen. In response to higher altitude, the body's diffusing capacity increases in order to be able to process more air. At high altitudes, the body undergoes several physiological adaptations to cope with reduced oxygen availability. These include acute hypoxia responses such as increased ventilation rate (hyperventilation) to enhance oxygen uptake, ventilatory acclimatization over days to weeks which further increases breathing efficiency, and hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction which redistributes blood flow to better-ventilated lung areas. Chronic exposure leads to increased red blood cell production (polycythemia) to improve oxygen transport. Additionally, altitude can cause pathologies like high-altitude pulmonary edema (HAPE), characterized by fluid accumulation in the lungs due to increased pulmonary artery pressure. These mechanisms collectively influence lung capacity and function at altitude. Practical methods to improve lung capacity at high altitudes include: engaging in regular aerobic exercise to enhance cardiovascular and respiratory efficiency; practicing specific breathing techniques such as diaphragmatic and pursed-lip breathing to increase oxygen intake; maintaining good posture to optimize lung expansion; undergoing gradual high-altitude training or acclimatization to stimulate physiological adaptations like increased red blood cell production and enhanced diffusing capacity; and avoiding smoking to prevent lung damage.

When someone living at or near sea level travels to locations at high altitudes (eg. the Andes, Denver, Colorado, Tibet, the Himalayas, etc.) they can develop a condition called altitude sickness because their lungs cannot respirate sufficiently in the thinner air.

Practical methods to measure lung capacity for athletes include spirometry tests such as forced vital capacity (FVC) and maximal voluntary ventilation (MVV), which assess lung function under exertion. Additionally, athlete-specific procedures involve using devices like spirometers or balloons to measure tidal volume, vital capacity, and inspiratory reserve volume. Step-by-step procedures include instructing the athlete to perform maximal inhalation and exhalation maneuvers, recording volumes, and comparing results to normative athlete data. Experimental methods such as body plethysmography and nitrogen washout can also be used for precise residual volume measurement. These methods help tailor lung capacity assessment to athletes' enhanced respiratory demands and training adaptations.

The tidal volume, vital capacity, inspiratory capacity and expiratory reserve volume can be measured directly with a spirometer. Spirometry is a pulmonary function test that measures lung volumes by having the patient perform controlled breathing maneuvers using a spirometer device. The test involves maximal inhalation followed by rapid, forceful exhalation into the spirometer, which records volumes such as Forced Vital Capacity (FVC) and Forced Expiratory Volume in one second (FEV1). These measurements help assess lung function by quantifying the amount and speed of air that can be inhaled and exhaled, providing insight into lung volumes and airflow obstruction or restriction. Determination of the residual volume can be done by radiographic planemetry, body plethysmography, closed circuit dilution and nitrogen washout. Smoking significantly impacts lung capacity and function, leading to conditions such as emphysema and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD). Pulmonary function tests (PFTs), including forced vital capacity (FVC) and forced expiratory volume in one second (FEV1), are commonly used to assess lung function in smokers. These tests help detect airflow obstruction and measure the degree of lung impairment caused by smoking. Smokers typically show reduced FEV1/FVC ratios, indicating obstructive lung disease. Regular lung capacity testing is crucial for early diagnosis and management of smoking-related pulmonary conditions.

These are the basic elements of a ventilatory pulmonary function test. The results (in particular FEV1/FVC and FRC) can be used to distinguish between restrictive and obstructive pulmonary diseases:

Type Examples Description FEV1/FVC
restrictive diseases pulmonary fibrosis volumes are decreased often in a normal range (0.8 - 1.0)

Other

The largest human lung capacity recorded is that of British rower Peter Reed at 11.68 litres, roughly twice that of an average person.
