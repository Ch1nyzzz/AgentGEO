What is a Business Plan? How To Write Yours in 10 Steps

Learn what a business plan is, what lenders and investors look for, and how to write one in 10 steps using a traditional or lean format.

Business plans do more than pave the way to secure loans or investments. While a great business plan meets lenders' and investors' expectations, it also helps entrepreneurs clarify business concepts, identify potential roadblocks, pinpoint target markets, devise growth plans, and more.

That's because researching and drafting your plan forces you to think through all the aspects of your business. At the end of the process, you'll have a clear vision of your business idea and a concrete course of action.

Ahead, learn more about what a business plan is and why you should make one, then use our step-by-step guide to help you write your own.

What is a business plan?

A business plan is a strategic document that describes a company, what it does, and where it wants to go. The plan outlines the business's short- and long-term goals and details the strategies and timelines for achieving them. It includes key elements like financial projections, a market analysis, and a detailed operations and logistics plan.

A business plan gives you a road map of what you want to achieve and how you'll get there. This clear direction provides confidence and bearings when things are tough. 

Often, financial institutions and investors need to see a business plan before funding a company. Even if you don't plan to seek outside funding, you can use a well-crafted plan to guide you as you launch and grow your own business.


Business plan types

Traditional business plan Lean business plan
Length Dozens of pages long 1-2 pages long
Content Comprehensive and detailed Only includes the most important information
Purpose Used to secure outside funding Used for internal purposes, like onboarding new hires


There are two main business plan formats: traditional and lean.

Traditional business plans

Covering everything from your finances to your marketing plan, a traditional business plan contains detailed information, often spanning many pages, including deep analysis of your competitors and target market.

Use a traditional business plan when applying for bank loans or presenting to venture capitalists. Formal institutions want to see due diligence and financial roadmaps when deciding on funding opportunities. 

However, traditional business plans can be time-consuming to research and write—and to read. This is where lean business plans have an advantage.

Lean business plans

A condensed version of a traditional business plan, you might use a lean business plan if you're writing a business plan for yourself and don't need to flesh out every detail.

Opt for a lean business plan when you want to validate an idea. Another option is when your startup is in a fluid period where the business model might change. 

This format could also be useful if you're writing for an audience that doesn't need to know certain information about your business. For example, new employees probably don't need to see in-depth financial data—give them a lean small business plan instead.

💡Tip: Condense your lean business plan into a business model canvas. This one-pager is a visual reference that helps readers envision your business model quickly. Learn the difference between the two.

4 things a business plan helps you achieve

Because investors and lenders rely on business plans to evaluate the viability of a business before deciding to fund it, plans are commonly associated with getting a business loan. There are government resources that provide free planning help for entrepreneurs, including the Small Business Administration, or SBA.

But that's not all you can do with a business plan: Business plans can also help entrepreneurs find weak spots in their business before they launch, potentially avoiding costly mistakes down the road.

"Laying out a business plan helped us identify the 'unknowns' and made it easier to spot the gaps where we'd need help or, at the very least, to skill up ourselves," says Jordan Barnett, owner of men's leggings company Kapow Meggings.

Here are a few additional reasons to consider writing a business plan:

1. Plan strategically

Writing a business plan can help you grasp the amount of time, money, and resources you'll need to build and grow your company. Surfacing these needs helps you adequately prepare for the future. A business plan also includes detailed financial projections revealing what will and won't be possible—finances-wise—in the future.

2. Evaluate ideas

When you research and write a business plan, you look at everything from market conditions to production logistics to expenses, which helps determine whether or not your business concept is currently viable.

If you have multiple business ideas, drafting a rough business plan for each examines which ones have the highest chances of success, allowing you to focus your time and energy on those with the most potential.


3. Clarify your selling points

Writing a business plan requires you to clarify your competitive advantage and brand pillars. These details help you pitch your company to both investors and consumers.

This effort lays the groundwork for your future brand strategy and marketing copy. If you plan to collaborate with other brands, you can also use these business plan elements to persuade other companies to partner with you, especially if you share similar missions and values.

4. Prepare for funding conversations

Business plans are also excellent for prepping you for a chat with investors or lenders. It provides proof to show your business is a safe investment, as your financial projections and growth strategy are all in one document. 

Backed by a well-researched plan, you are ready to talk about how much your company is worth and how much money you want. 

How to write a business plan in 10 steps

  1. Choose your business plan format and template
  2. Think about your audience
  3. Write a company overview
  4. List your products and services
  5. Perform a market analysis
  6. Devise a marketing plan
  7. Provide a logistics and operations plan
  8. Make a financial plan
  9. Draft an executive summary
  10. Update and revise your business plan

Writing a traditional business plan (like the one outlined below) allows you to think through each element of your business idea.

Here's your step-by-step guide to create a business plan.

1. Choose your business plan format and template

First, choose the format that best serves your purpose. If you're creating a business plan to show potential lenders and investors, you'll probably want to use the traditional business plan. If you're creating a business plan to help you think through your ideas (or to share with employees or potential partners for a brand collaboration), you might opt for the shorter lean business plan.

Next, find a good business plan template. Shopify's free template includes both a traditional and lean business plan template, as well as examples. These help you get a clearer sense of what your finished product should look like.

Some entrepreneurs use AI tools like ChatGPT to draft sections of their business plan faster. If you do, treat AI output as a first draft—verify facts, tailor voice and tone to your brand, and add specific details only you know.


2. Think about your audience

Before you start writing the contents of your business plan, put yourself in the mind of your audience. What are they going to be looking for in your business plan? For example, new hires might care more about your brand values, while investors might care more about your financials and points of differentiation in the market.

Imagine their possible objections or concerns. Get in front of those potential roadblocks by proactively addressing them in your plan.

3. Write a company overview

Your company overview should answer two essential questions: "Who are you?" and "What do you plan to do?" When a reader finishes this section of your business plan, they should have a clear idea of your company.

Here are the components you should include in your company overview:

Business name, location, and legal structure

Introduce your business by name, and give any relevant back story on your business name. Note your headquarters location, any satellite offices or remote work, and where it will do business (if different from the first two). Finally, note your legal business structure. For example, is your company a sole proprietorship? An LLC? An S corp?

Business concept, model, and industry

Briefly describe what your company does and indicate your business model. There are five main types: business to consumer (B2C), direct to consumer (DTC), business to business (B2B), consumer to consumer (C2C), and consumer to business (C2B).

Finally, note your industry, and how your niche. Will you disrupt an entrenched industry with cutting edge technology or new innovative business practices? Or will you create a new category of product within an industry?

Competitive advantage

A competitive advantage is what your small business has that others don't. That could be lower prices (cost leadership strategy), higher quality (differentiation strategy), or branding that appeals to a particular group (focus strategy).

Mission and values

Craft a concise mission statement that explains why your company exists and what it aims to do. These are public-facing, idealistic statements, and they're often just one to two lines long.

Then, outline your brand values, asking yourself, "What impact do I want my business to have on the world?" and "How do I want my company to treat its customers and employees?"

Short- and long-term business objectives

What do you hope to achieve in the next year, and what do you hope to achieve in the next one to five years? Make your objectives achievable by using the SMART goal-setting framework (specific, measurable, attainable, realistic, and time-bound).

Leadership and organizational structure

Introduce key members of your management team (even if that's just you as a solopreneur) with brief descriptions of credentials and professional backgrounds. If you have employees or partners, list each person's roles and responsibilities along with their salaries. Detail your company's organizational structure, too.

4. List your products and services

At this point, you'll have mentioned your products and services a few times in your business plan. This is where you'll detail them. List each product or service you currently sell or plan to sell in the future alongside each product's key features and price.

Instead of simply listing your product prices, however, include your pricing strategy. This is where you'll explain how you determined the price of each product.

A strong pricing strategy considers your costs, your audience's willingness to pay, and your long-term company goals while also considering how price might fit into your value proposition. Explain the rationale behind your prices (beyond simply maximizing revenue), and list any reasons why your prices are higher than competitors, if so.

Describe new products you'll launch in the near future and any intellectual property you own. Express how they'll improve profitability. It's also important to note where products are coming from—handmade crafts are sourced differently than trending products for a dropshipping business, for instance.

5. Perform a market analysis

Market analysis is an especially key section of your business plan. This research helps determine the viability of your product, identify your intended audience, and unlock your competitive advantage. It will also be one of your longest sections. Learn advanced techniques in our guide to market segmentation.

Here's what you'll need to do:

  • Analyze market size, growth, and trends. Detail the size of your market and any forecasted growth. Note any technological, economic, and social trends.
  • Look for market opportunities. Identify market gaps or new opportunities linked to growing trends.
  • Identify your target market. Use market research to identify your target market, including demographic info (age, gender, socioeconomic status, location, and education), purchasing habits (if they buy similar products and how often), and decision drivers (the pain points and values that guide their purchasing decision).
  • Estimate market size. Perform an analysis of your total addressable market to approximate the size of your target market; this clarifies how big of an opportunity there is for your startup business.
  • Conduct a competitive analysis. Analyze your competitors' products, pricing, marketing strategies, and market share (use our competitive analysis template and AI tools to streamline this research).
  • Conduct a SWOT analysis. List your business's strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats (SWOT).

6. Devise a marketing plan

You might sell the best products in the world, but no one will buy them if they don't know about them. That's why it's vital to think about your marketing strategy, even at this early stage of planning a business. This section of your plan functions as a marketing road map as you move forward.

Keep your target market top of mind as you develop your marketing strategy. These are the customers you're trying to attract to your business, so make sure your marketing tactics appeal to them, and that you choose marketing platforms your target market uses.

If you're thinking about investing heavily in Instagram marketing and TikTok ads, for example, verify whether or not your target audience actually uses Instagram and TikTok.

Here's what you'll include in this section of your business plan:

Product positioning and brand messaging

Product positioning is the foundation of your marketing plan. Give a concise explanation of your product's value proposition to your target market. Think about how you want your target market to think and feel about your product, and how your product compares to its competitors.

Then, outline the key brand messages you'll use to communicate that value proposition to the world. Give proof points and reasons to believe in your value proposition, as well as specific guidelines about how to talk about the features and benefits of your products. This will become the foundation of your marketing copy.

📚Learn: How to define and communicate your competitive advantage through product positioning.

Acquisition channels

Your strategy for acquisition channels is how you'll find new customers. These channels might include paid advertising, public relations campaigns, content marketing, and social media marketing, for example.

Specific platforms and tactics

Once you've determined your broader acquisition channels (say, paid advertising), clarify how you'll use each channel. For example, maybe you decide to advertise on local billboards and run search engine optimization (SEO) campaigns targeting strategic keywords.

Tools and technology

List the tools you'll need to execute your marketing strategy, like marketing automation software, a social media scheduling app, or a customer relationship management (CRM) software.

Goals and evaluation

How will you measure marketing success? Explain what you want your marketing efforts to achieve, like higher average order value (AOV), more social media followers, or increased website traffic.


7. Provide a logistics and operations plan

Logistics and operations are the workflows you'll implement to make your business idea a reality. Thinking through this section helps you identify holes in your business concept and pinpoint the resources you'll need to bring your idea to life.

A high-quality logistics and operations section should signal to your reader you have a solid understanding of your supply chain and strong contingency plans in business to cover potential uncertainty. If possible, add back-up options for elements like suppliers—for example, what happens if your supplier goes out of business? What if tariffs quadruple? Or a catastrophic weather event impacts the factory?

Here's what to include in this section of your business plan:

Suppliers

Where do you source your products (or the raw ingredients for your products)? For example, do you buy finished products from a wholesaler or dropshipping partner? If so, which ones? Do you plan to import or export as part of your business? Do you source raw materials from farms? If you do, list the farms.

Production

How are your products made? Explain the processes that turn raw materials into finished products, including information on the technologies and facilities (like specialized factories) needed to make your products.

Shipping and fulfillment

Detail how your products will end up in customers' hands. List shipping carriers and detail how your products will be stored. For example, maybe you store and ship products from your own home, or maybe you use a third-party fulfillment partner.

Inventory management

How much inventory will you keep on hand, how will you keep track of it, and how will you make sure you don't run out (or have too much)? Detail your inventory management strategies and list any inventory management software you plan to use.

8. Make a financial plan

No matter how great your idea is—and regardless of the effort, time, and money you invest—a business depends on its financial health. If you're seeking an SBA loan, you'll also need to identify your business's NAICS code—a classification system lenders and government agencies use to understand your industry. Visit the SBA's NAICS lookup tool to find yours.

A good financial plan draws a road map to profitability and assures investors and lenders your business is a smart investment choice.

The level of detail you need in your financial plan depends on your audience and goals.

If you've been in business for a while and you're updating your business plan for investors or lenders, include your financial statements:

  • Balance sheet. A record of your business's assets and liabilities.
  • Cash flow statement. The cash flowing in and out of your business over a certain time period.
  • Income statement. Also known as a profit and loss statement, this shows you the company's income and expenditures over a specific period of time.

Regardless of how long you've been in business, consider creating sales forecasts that go several years into the future—up to the next five years.


Revenue and sales projections

Create a projection of the income that will flow into your business (e.g., from sales). If you're just starting up, you'll need to make estimates based on forecasted sales; if you've been in business for some time, use historical data.

Expense forecasts and burn rate

An account of the money you expect to flow out of your business.

Business capital

Outline the capital you have on hand—for example, any capital you're contributing yourself or from friends and family—and/or the capital you plan to raise.

Funding request and use of funds

State your funding requirements for the next five years if you’re seeking outside capital. Explain how you’ll use the money—for equipment, working capital, or debt repayment—and specify the type of funding you need.

Supporting documents for your appendix

Collect materials that reinforce your financial statements and projections. Include credit histories, letters of intent, contracts, and legal documents that provide evidence for your plan.

For step-by-step guidance, see this complete guide to creating financial projections for your business plan.

9. Draft an executive summary

The executive summary is actually the first section of your business plan—but it's the last one you should write. Because it highlights the most important information in each section, it's easiest to draft once you've conducted all of your research.

The executive summary distills all the information that follows and gives time-crunched reviewers a high-level overview of your business that persuades them to read further.

Your business plan's executive summary should include the following elements:

  • Mission statement, from your company overview
  • Business concept, from your company overview
  • Business objectives, from your company overview
  • Product description, from your products and services offered
  • Competitive advantage, from your market analysis
  • Target market, from your market analysis
  • Financial projections, from your financial plan
  • An ask for potential investors (source this information from the funding and investments section of your financial plan and tailor the it to the specific investor or lender)

10. Update and revise your business plan

Business plans aren't static documents. As the market changes and your business grows, you'll want a plan that provides you with accurate information and a realistic road map.

If you're a more established company, consider updating your business plan at least one per year. If you're a newer, faster-growing company, you might want to revise it as frequently as every quarter.

As you grow, compare your plan's projections against actual performance—this 'plan vs. actual' analysis reveals what's working and where you need to pivot. Many entrepreneurs use simple spreadsheets or business planning software to track variances monthly or quarterly.

If you're conducting regular quarterly or annual updates, think of your quarterly reviews as quick updates and your yearly ones as big overhauls. As you revise, use data to inform your changes. For example, use marketing metrics like social media engagement and return on ad spend (ROAS) to guide changes to your marketing plan.

Review period Revision
Annual
  • Full review of each element of your financial plan
  • Market analysis
  • Long-term goals
  • Product lineup
  • SWOT analysis
Quarterly
  • Short-term business objectives
  • Quarterly financial projections
  • Marketing strategy


Sometimes, however, changes outside of your control mandate a more immediate update. Here are some external factors that might require a business plan revision:

  • Changing competitors. New players enter your market, or existing competitors have increased or decreased their market share.
  • Changing consumer trends. Buyers are more or less interested in the products you're selling.
  • Supplier changes. Your raw materials are more or less expensive than they used to be.
  • Government regulations. Depending on your industry and products, navigating tariffs and changes to regulatory policies can impact your business.

Here are a few internal changes that might signal it's time for a new business plan:

  • Headcount growth. You've onboarded a large number of new employees.
  • New products. You started selling new products or services.
  • Large financial changes. Your funding has shifted with a loan or new investment or your revenue and expenses changed significantly.

Red flags that weaken a business plan

As you refine your document, watch out for these common issues that can diminish its value:

  • Unrealistic projections. Financial forecasts that don't use market data reduce credibility.
  • Vague goals. Objectives without measurable outcomes make progress difficult to track.
  • Ignoring competition. Strategy documents that overlook rivals lack market context.
  • Lack of customer focus. Prioritizing product features over audience needs can limit sales.

Prepare your business plan today

Even if you don't plan to pitch investors, a business plan helps you identify clear next steps for your business and reveal gaps in your business idea before they become issues.

Whether you're working on starting a new online business idea, building a retail storefront, growing your established business, or purchasing an existing business, you now understand how to write a business plan that suits your business goals and needs.

Business plan FAQ

How do I write an effective business plan?

Learning how to write dea business plan is simple if you use a business plan template or business plan software. Typically, a traditional business plan for every new business should have the following components:

  • Executive summary
  • Company overview
  • Market analysis
  • Products and services offered
  • Marketing plan
  • Logistics and operations
  • Financial plan

What is a good business plan?

A good business plan offers a clear company description, goals, growth plan, and operational plan. It starts with a strong executive summary, then details information like competitive advantage, target market, marketing tactics, and financial projections.

A business plan template helps ensure you include all the necessary information in the correct format.

What are the 3 main purposes of a business plan?

The three main purposes of a business plan are:

  1. To clarify your plans for growth
  2. To understand your financial needs
  3. To attract funding from outside stakeholders or secure a business loan

What can I do with a completed business plan?

Beyond securing funding, you can use your business plan to test new ideas, onboard employees, attract partners, monitor cash flow, and guide strategic decisions. Many startup entrepreneurs revisit their plan quarterly to track progress and adjust tactics.

What are the different types of business plans?

There are two main types of business plans: traditional and lean. A traditional business plan is longer and written in paragraphs, while a lean business plan is shorter and often features bulleted lists. Shopify's free business plan template includes both. Some entrepreneurs also use a business model canvas—a one-page visual framework that maps your value proposition, customer segments, and revenue streams.

If you plan to sell baked goods, for example, you might consider a traditional option for your bakery business plan. Bakeries sometimes require more planning because of health codes and perishable ingredients.

Compared to starting a clothing line, where you may choose a lightweight version. A clothing line business plan could be more simple, especially if you contain it to selling products from wholesale clothing vendors in the US or a print-on-demand model.
