How to Care for Hyacinth Plants Indoors

By Jenny Green, ContributorUpdated Dec 2, 2018 1:59 a.m.

Hyacinths (Hyacinthus orientalis) add a splash of color to a bright windowsill. Caring for hyacinths doesn't require much effort because the plants grow from bulbs. Give hyacinth plants moisture and a cool spot, and they'll provide fragrant blooms in shades of red, pink, purple and blue with little other care. For indoor flowers during winter, purchase pre-chilled hyacinth bulbs and follow the procedure known as "forcing."

How to Force Hyacinth Bulbs

Pre-chilled hyacinth bulbs require a period of cool temperatures and darkness to trigger them into growth. Fill a 3-inch plastic container with bulb fiber, and push a bulb into the fiber so that the bulb is covered about halfway. Sprinkle enough water over the fiber to moisten it, and place the container in a dark place that's cool but doesn't freeze, like a garage, cellar or refrigerator. However, don't place the bulbs near apples, which produce a gas that makes the bulbs rot. Check the bulbs every week or so, and sprinkle the compost with water if it's dry. When green shoots have appeared and grown about 2 inches long, the plants are ready for repotting into decorative containers and bringing out into the house.

Warning

Wear rubber gloves when handling hyacinth bulbs if your skin is sensitive. The bulbs release tiny, needle-like crystals that can irritate.

Containers and Compost for Hyacinth Plants

Most indoor plants grow best in potting compost and in containers that have drainage holes, but hyacinth plants are a special case. With care, hyacinth bulbs thrive in forcing jars that contain only water, and gravel-filled, shallow, bulb-growing bowls without drainage holes. Forcing jars have narrow necks, where the bulb sits around 1/4 inch above the water below. The plant's roots dangle in the water, but the water should never touch the base of the bulb. Hyacinth plants also grow well in a mixture of two parts regular potting compost and one part coarse grit or horticultural sand, or plain gravel. If your container for growing your hyacinth plants has no drainage holes, spread a 1-inch-deep layer of bulb fiber on the base to absorb excess water. Space the bulbs 3 to 4 inches apart in their new containers and at half the depth of the bulb in their growing medium.

Where to Grow Hyacinths in the Home

A cool spot in bright, indirect light in the home encourages the best, longest-lasting blooms from hyacinth plants. Hyacinths are hardy outdoors in U.S. Department of Agriculture hardiness zones 4 through 8. Indoors, the recommended temperature range is between 60 degrees and 65 degrees Fahrenheit, so avoid placing the plants near a heat source during winter. A bright, airy spot near a window that receives indirect sunlight is ideal for these plants. To avoid a pronounced lean towards the window, rotate the container a quarter turn every day.

Watering and Fertilizing Indoor Hyacinth Plants

The key to successfully growing hyacinth plants indoors is to provide moisture without soaking the bulbs, which causes rotting. The bulbs contain plenty of stored nutrients for the leaves and flowers, which makes fertilizer unnecessary. The potting compost mixture or gravel should only remain moist and never soaked. Push a finger 1 inch beneath the surface to check that the growing medium is dry to that depth before lightly watering, avoiding the plants' leaves. Adding liquid fertilizer for indoor plants to the water at the dilution stated on the label may prolong the blooming period.

Pests and Diseases That Affect Indoor Hyacinths

Hyacinth plants rarely suffer from pests or diseases, and growing them indoors is no exception. Some common indoor plant pests that might infest them include mealybugs, which are white, cottony or waxy insects; scale, which are small insects like limpet shells; and fungus gnats, which are tiny flies. Mealybugs and scale are fairly easy to spot on hyacinth leaves. If you do see some, pick them off with your fingers. Fungus gnats thrive in moist organic matter, so reduce watering if you see these little flies. Excessive watering can also cause root rot. The hyacinth plants' leave and stems wilt, and the plant dies. There's a small chance they could recover if the overwatering is stopped immediately. Other potential diseases like gray mold and powdery mildew are caused by unsuitable growing conditions. Move the plants to an airy, bright spot and prune the affected foliage, dipping the pruning shears in rubbing alcohol afterward to sanitize them.

Tips

  • In order for your hyacinths to bloom indoors over the winter, plant them in late September.
  • The best soil mix for this project is 3 parts loam, 2 parts peat moss and 1 part sand, but a loose potting mix will work.
  • Store bulbs in a cool place until planting.
  • Plant only firm, healthy bulbs.
  • If you plant a few planters over several weeks, you can have a continual bloom of hyacinths. Mark the date you planted each planter so you know when to remove it from the refrigerator.
  • If the flower stem appears to be trapped within the leaves, place your planter in a dark, cool location for two or three days. This forces the flowers to extend up out of the leaf cluster.
