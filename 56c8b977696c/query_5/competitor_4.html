Difference Between Office Supplies & Expenses for Tax Purposes

By Danielle Smyth, Contributor

Opening and running a business costs money, whether spent on startup costs, overhead, wages, or office supplies as simple as notepads. There are three major kinds of office expense categories to consider: general office expenses, office supplies and large office equipment such as furniture. All need to be dealt with properly when companies prepare their business taxes to receive deductions.

General Office Expenses vs. Supplies

General office expenses are related to office operations. Your general office expenses list might include desktop and laptop computers and tablets, office phone systems and employee cellphones, accounting software, website services and internet fees. Other operational expenses may include cleaning services and utilities.

Office supplies are short-term items that have to be refilled or replaced. Inline Accounting advises that, depending on the type of business, they include printer ink, toner, coffee, staples, pens, water and stationery, including paper invoices. Large items that cost more money, such as office furniture, are considered to be business equipment.

Business Expenses vs. Capital Expenditures

Capital expenditures are monies used by businesses to obtain, maintain and improve major physical assets. These expenditures occur when companies make major investments, expand operations, or begin large projects. These could include building a new warehouse, installing a new roof, or purchasing a fleet of trucks.

These are a different category from business expenses. Capital expenditures are for long-term business needs, while business expenses are more short-term. Capital expenditures cannot be fully deducted in the same way as office supplies and expenses.

General IRS Business Expense Regulations

Freshbooks explains that business expenses such as office supplies can be deducted from tax returns if the business is in operation to make a profit. In general, business expenses have to be "both ordinary and necessary." In other words, they have to be expected costs for running the type of business and needed for day-to-day and long-term operations.

Business expenses must be separated from capital expenditures, expenses used to determine the cost of goods sold, and personal expenses. In general, you can deduct the total amount of any business expense if it meets that "ordinary and necessary" criteria and is not a capital expense.

The IRS has different rules for businesses that are home-based. These business owners must distinguish between items that are used exclusively for the business and those that are not. A laptop computer that is used for children's schoolwork cannot be classified as a business expense. If it is used for business some of the time, you may be able to deduct a portion of certain expenses, however.

What's on Business Expenses Lists

A business that is not located in a home may be able to deduct a variety of business expenses. Large companies may have more qualifying deductions than small ones. A big company's business expenses list might include rent, insurance, equipment rental, bank fees, payroll processing fees, employee health care and benefit programs, and legal fees.

Small companies may also be able to deduct many of these costs. Businesses that employ salespeople may be able to deduct costs for company cars, travel, accommodations and training. According to Ageras, additional expenses related to maintenance, repair and uniforms may also be deducted.

The list of qualifying office expenses for home-based businesses is a bit different. There must be an area in the home used exclusively for running the business. This business expenses list may include a portion of the home's property taxes and mortgage interest, a separate phone line used only for the company, insurance, a security system, home maintenance, and upkeep and repairs.

Small Office Expense Deductions

To claim any office expense deductions, the business must keep itemized receipts. When the business files its tax forms, it deducts office supplies from the profit. So, a company that earns a $100,000 profit and spends $15,000 on office supplies has a $85,000 profit for tax purposes, unless there are other deductions.

General and administrative business expenses are ordinarily deductible in the same year that they are paid. There is an exception for costs directly related to starting a new business; these must be capitalized over a five-year period.

There are rules for how things like entertainment expenses and client gifts can be deducted. For example, business owners can deduct 50 percent of a client meal, but this is limited to only one person; group meals and parties do not count. For client gifts, a 25 percent deduction is permitted.

Home Office Expense Deductions

The physical home office space can be deducted in one of two ways: simplified or standard. The IRS describes the deduction for the simplified method as "$5 per square foot of office space up to 300 square feet." The standard method is more complicated; it tracks actual expenses and calculates a final number based on the percentage of the home that is used for business.

Other home office expense deductions are also calculated as percentages based on the office space or mileage. These include transportation, internet service, phone use, and other items.

Ways to Categorize Expenses

Business expenses can be further broken down into three categories: fixed, variable and periodic. Fixed costs remain the same but may change a bit at a certain time, such as a year. An obvious example of this is a monthly rent payment that increases after 12 months.

Variable expenses are not consistent and can account for a business's largest expenses. These can be seen in payroll costs, utility bills, inventory purchases and new product launches. Periodic expenses are unexpected and can be difficult to plan for. Having to redo a large, incorrect order, replace a computer system, or open a new office are all part of this.

How Does Depreciation Work?

The IRS allows small business owners to write off the first $18,000 paid for equipment bought for business use only in a given year. If a business normally buys this much equipment every year, it may want to take advantage of the full tax break right away.

If it is more of a variable or periodic cost, the business owner may want to take advantage of depreciation. This is the reduction in an asset's value with the passage of time. Depreciation is tax-deductible for business owners and is based on an IRS formula. It allows the person who is filing to depreciate the purchase cost over time to spread it out.

Several kinds of office expenses may be classified as fixed assets on balance sheets and can be depreciated. Companies use fixed assets to earn long-term income, and these assets stay with the company for longer periods. Examples include company vehicles, furniture, computers and machinery.

Helpful Resources for Tax Filing

The IRS has a lot of information on its website that can help business owners with tax preparation. IRS Publication 535 on business expenses has guidelines for topics that include rent expenses, paying employees, amortization and depletion.
