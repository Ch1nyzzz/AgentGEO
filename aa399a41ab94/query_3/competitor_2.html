Messier Objects

    Many fuzzy objects (nebula) are visible in the night sky, and are included in the catalog of Messier Objects, which the 18th century French astronomer Charles Messier compiled to aid him in his search for comets.
    The above image of fuzzy objects shows several components of the Milky Way's matter superimposed on an image of the Lagoon Nebula.
    • A - North American Nebula - emission nebula
    • B - Jewel Box - open cluster
    • C - 47 Tucanae - globular cluster
    • D - Cygnus Loop - supernova remnant


    Nebulae
    • emission nebula - glowing gas and dust
      • Orion nebula
    • absorption nebula - obscures vision
      • Horsehead nebula
    • reflection nebula - reflects starlight, no emitted radiation
      • Pleiades
    • planetary nebula - formed by matter ejected by red giant stars - emitted light
      • Ring nebula
      • Eskimo nebula
      • Cat's Key nebula
    • supernova remnants - remains from violent explosion of a star
      . -> often leaves neutron star (pulsar)
      • Crab nebula
    • spiral "nebula" - now known to reside outside the Milky Way Galaxy
      . -> other galaxies (this was not realized at the time of Messier)
      • Andromeda

Structure of the Milky Way

    In visible light we see an accumulation of stars, but the structure is not so clear
    • In the late 18th Century, long before distances to stars were known, William Herschel tried to estimate the size and shape of our Galaxy
      • Note - it was not even known then that there were galaxies outside the Milky Way
    • He recorded all the stars, and created a map of the Galaxy.
      • He did not realize most of the stars were obscured from his view
      • He concluded the Galaxy was a flattened, disk-shaped, with the Sun near the center
      • He greatly under estimated of the size of the Galaxy.


    In infrared, or far-infrared the structure of a disk and a bulge starts to be revealed
    Subdivisions

    • Galactic Bulge
      • 6 kpc (about 20,000 light-years) across
        4 kpc (about 12,000 light-years) perpendicular to the plane
      • Composed of old stars (population II) and young stars (population I)
      • shaped like football, about half as wide as long


    • Galactic Disk
      • 30 kpc (about 100,000 light-years) diameter
        "only" 300 p.c. (about 1,000 light-years) thickness
      • old stars (population II) and young stars (population I), gas, and dust
      • Spiral arms


    • Galactic Halo
      • Spherical shape
      • Includes globular clusters
        • which contain only old stars (population II)
      • Radius of about 65,000 light years
      • Absence of gas or dust


    • Dark Halo
      • Radius of 200,000 or 300,000 light years
      • Contains 80%-95% of the mass of the galaxy (dark matter)
        What is this Dark matter?
        • More on this later!

  • younger, metal-rich stars

Population II Stars

  • older, metal-poor stars

  • contains newer stars (population I - like the Sun)
    • and older stars

Galactic Halo

  • contains mostly old stars (population II)

Measuring the Milky Way

    How can we measure the distance to objects in the sky?
    Stellar Parallax:
    1/2 angle through which a star's position shifts as earth orbits the sun.
    (actually this only works in determining stellar distances for nearby stars.)
      Nearest Stars:
    • Alpha Centauri complex (triple-star system)
      • Proxima Centauri at 1.3 pc (4.3 ly)
        • 0.76 arc-secs
    • Barnard's Star
      • 1.8 pc (6.0 ly)
        • 0.55 arc-secs
    Since stellar parallax fails beyond 100 parsecs or so, we need other methods
    Standard Candle
    If we know how bright something really is (known as Luminosity or Absolute Brightness) then we can determine from how bright it appears (Apparent Brightness) how far away it is
      This follows simply from the inverse square law:

    Pulsating variable stars
    • RR Lyrae variables
      • characteristic light curve
      • periods of 0.5 to 1 day
    • Cepheid variables
      • characteristic light curve
      • periods of 1 to 100 days
    The pulsation period of each of these stars depends on luminosity

    Therefore, a measurement of the pulsation period immediately tells us the star's luminosity
    we can see its apparent brightness
    and from the inverse square law, we can determine the distance

    This is a standard candle, which allows us to measure distances out to about 25 million parsecs (25 Mpc, or Megaparsecs)

Orbital motion of the stars in the Milky Way

    In the disk of the Milky Way, stars and other matter are rotating around the center in a regular pattern, as revealed by Doppler effects

    In the galactic halo and the galactic bulge, the motion is largely random

Tracing the Milky Way's history

  • The oldest stars (in the globular clusters) orbit the galaxy in elongated elliptical orbits.
  • Galaxy must have been born from huge gas cloud
  • Where are the first stars without heavy metals?

Very important clue on evolution of galaxy:

    The lower the metal abundance (pop I vs. pop II), the farther the objects are found from the plane of the disk
    (remember, low metal abundance (pop II) means old)
    • stars are born with elemental abundance's of gases of birth
    • stars inherit orbital motion abut galaxy of parent cloud
    • massive stars evolve quickly and spew heavy elements into interstellar medium

Milky Way has formed as it is through Evolution

  • First stars were formed in a very large region of space, with matter spread out in irregular patterns
  • First stars (population II) formed more than 10 billion years ago, and collapsed into globular clusters.
  • Huge region of clouds of primordial gas and early stars collapsed into large cloud of matter
  • Galaxy continued to Evolve, as slowly spinning cloud collapsed into disk and dense core
  • Leaving behind stars formed early as a halo
  • Disk rich in gas and dust formed, from which the Sun emerged about 5 billion years ago.

Is the Milky Way a Spiral Galaxy?

    We observe many spiral galaxies.
    • (NOTE: stars exist between arms - bright (O & B) stars are found "only" in the arms)
    How can we look for spiral structure? - or how do we map out the arms?
    1. galactic (or open) clusters
      • (found in spiral arms)
    2. O & B stars
      • bright, short lived
    3. Distribution of gas based on HI 21 cm line Doppler measurements
      • HI is the astronomer's name for hydrogen atoms
      • when the electron "flips its spin", it emits a 21 cm radio wave
      • (radio frequency passes through dust)
    4. HII regions
      • HII ionized Hydrogen
      • (found preferentially in spiral arms)
    5. Molecular clouds ( CO)
    Methods 1, 2, and 4 use visible light, and are limited by dust blockage
    Conclusion:
      There is evidence for arms

A Puzzle of the Arms

    Why don't they wrap around the MW?
    • 225,000,000 years for the Sun to circle the Milky Way
    • age of sun is about 4,500,000,000 years

    Therefore - the sun has been around the Milky Way 20 times.
      If the arms had been around this many times they would have wound up.
      • differential rotation
      But - arms are formed by new stars
      • which fade away in 107-108 years

      So

      Why don't the arms wrap up around the galaxy?
        Sun takes 225,000,000 years to orbit
      • However, arms fade away
        • brightest stars (O & B stars) burn out (and explode as supernovae) in about 10,000,000 years
        • longer living stars (like the G star known as our Sun) and emission nebulae drift away from the bright region of star formation over many millions of years

Theories of Arm Formation

    1. Density wave theory
        Density waves move through galaxy compressing matter as it passes and setting off star formation
        like traffic jam formation on highway
    2. self-propagating star formation (or Supernovae chain reaction)
        Supernovae send out waves which creates new stars and subsequent supernovae.

    • So stars are formed in specific regions that appear as arms
      • and over time the regions fade as stars burn out, or drift away

The Mass of the Milky Way Galaxy

    Weighing the galaxy
    • The orbital speed of a star or gas cloud around the Galaxy is determined by mass inside the orbit
        Calculation of the mass inside the Sun's orbit
          Kepler's Law: total mass = (orbital size)3 / (orbital period)2
          • orbital size in AU
          • orbital period in years (225,000,000)
          orbital size = 8000 pc x 206,000 AU / pc
          total mass ( within 8 kpc) = 9 x 1010 solar masses
          or 90 billion times the mass of the Sun

    • Luminous matter of Milky Way extends to about 15 kpc
          luminous matter (stars and gas) with 15 kpc directly measured to be about 6 x 1010solar masses
          or 60 billion times the mass of the Sun
        Calculation of mass within region of spiral structure and globular clusters (within about 15 kpc)
          total mass (within 15 kpc) = 2 x 1011 solar masses
          or 200 billion times the mass of the Sun

    • rotation curve for the Milky Way Galaxy
      • this implies our Galaxy is much larger than the luminous region
        Calculation of mass beyond visible structure (within about 40 kpc)
          total mass (within 40 kpc) = 6 x 1011 solar masses
          or 600 billion times the mass of the Sun

    • the luminous region is surrounded by an extensive, invisible dark halo
    • the dark halo contains most of the mass of the Milky Way, in the form of invisible Dark Matter

    Dark Matter
      Our galaxy is much larger than the size of the luminous portion (globular clusters and disk)
      Dark matter halo dwarfs the inner halo of stars and gas
      • estimate 2/3 of mass within the luminous region is dark matter
      Most of the mass of the Galaxy is invisible dark matter, which we do not understand

    • candidates for explanation of Dark Matter
      • Stellar Dark Matter
        • black holes
            rare but could make up some of dark matter
        • brown dwarfs
          • low-mass prestellar objects, never stars
        • white dwarfs
          • relic low mass stellar objects, no longer generating energy
        • faint, low-mass red dwarfs
          • Hubble Space Telescope examined a globular cluster with extreme sensitiviy
            and did not see the red dwarfs, so they are ruled out
      • exotic subatomic particles
        • WIMPs (Weakly interacting massive particles)
        • direct detection of WIMPs are being searched for in underground detectors
        • the Large Hadron Collider is attempting to produce WIMPs in proton-proton collisions
      • modified law of gravity at large distances

    • There may be more than one species of dark matter
      • for example, there may be many brown dwarfs and low mass stars (invisible) within inner parts of galaxies
      • while the dark matter further out might be composed of exotic particles

    • Lensing Search for Stellar Dark Matter
        gravitational lensing
        brightening of a star during a lensing event
        The objects observed cannot account for most of the dark matter

Center of the Milky Way

    Direction from Earth known from globular clusters
    • Sagittarius
      • clouds of gas and dust obscure view
      • cannot see into center with visible light
        • largest optical telescopes can see about 1/10 the distance to the center
      • can use infrared light, radio, x-rays, gamma-rays
    Radio
    • 1930's Karl Jansky
      • detected static on his receiver at approximately the same time each day (just 4 minutes earlier each day)
      • this turned out to be radio noise from the center of the galaxy which his "directional" antenna pointed at once a day.
    • Very intense radio emission coming from center
      • For example, very large array ( VLA) in New Mexico (27 radio telescopes)
        • produces detailed images
        • region of Galactic Center in radio spectrum
        • apparent ring of rotating matter of a few parsecs
    Infrared
    • IRAS (Infrared Astronomical Satellite)
    • very intense source from center of MW
    • brightness = 80,000,000 suns (bright)
    • size = 10 light years (compact)
    gamma-rays
    • intense (-rays from matter/anti-matter annihilation)
    • corresponds to ten billion tons of anti-matter annihilation per second
    X-rays
    • again - very intense emission
    Also - stars are found to be more closely packed.
    • Distance between about 1/300 the distance between the Sun and nearby stars.

Black Hole at the center of the Milky Way?

    Stars near the Galactic Center are found to be in orbits consistent with those expected when orbiting an mass of about 4 million solar masses.
    Many Astronomers believe a supermassive black hole is the only explanation for the data.
    What is a black hole?
    • A region of space in which matter has collapsed to the point where nothing can escape - not even light!
    Many astronomers believe the mass equivalent to about 4,000,000 suns has collapsed to the center of the MW.
    Then as matter continues to be pulled in, large amounts of radiation will be emitted.

Summary

  • There are many types of nebulae in the Milky Way,
    • but the spiral nebulae are galaxies beyond the Milky Way.
  • The Milky Way is a spiral galaxy.
  • The Milky Way is subdivided into a galactic bulge, a disk and a halo.
  • Globular clusters inhabit the halo.
  • Population I and population II stars appear in different regions.
  • Dark matter makes up a large fraction of the matter in the Milky Way.
  • We cannot see the center of the Milky Way with visible light, but infrared, radio, x-rays, and gamma-rays reveal its features.
  • It is likely that a giant (4,000,000 solar masses) black hole lives at the Milky Way's center.
  • Solar System (sun and planets) orbits Milky Way every 225,000,000 years.
  • Arms of spiral galaxies are bright and bluish largely due to brightest stars (O & B), as well as emission nebulae.
  • The Milky Way was formed by the collapse of a large region of gas and dust.
